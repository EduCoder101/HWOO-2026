Lesson 7 · Physical Features of Sydney

How physical geography shaped where Sydney grew
Lesson at a Glance
  • Explicit teaching of topography (the shape of the land surface) and relief (how maps show elevation using contour lines and colour shading) with examples from the atlas relief map of NSW
  • Comparison of Google Maps terrain view with the NSW relief map – what does each reveal that the other doesn’t?
  • Comparison of early Sydney maps (from the State Library of NSW) with modern maps – observe how the physical landscape shaped early settlement patterns and how those patterns persist today
  • Paired investigation: students annotate a physical features map with captions explaining how each feature (harbour, rivers, coastline, escarpment) influenced settlement. Captions must include reasoning, not just description.
  • Sharing and synthesis under document camera: which physical features had the biggest influence on Sydney’s growth?

Learning Intention

Describe the physical geography that has shaped Sydney’s development

Success Criteria

  • Identify Sydney’s major physical features: harbour, rivers, coastline, escarpment
  • Explain how at least one physical feature has influenced where people settled
  • Read and interpret a basic topographic relief map

Lesson Structure

1

Topography and Relief

Whole class

Introduce two new terms: topography is the shape of the land surface – the hills, valleys, plains, plateaus, and water bodies that make up a landscape. Relief is how maps show differences in elevation using contour lines and colour shading. This is genuinely new visual literacy; students have not read elevation data from maps before.

Show the NSW relief/topographic map from the atlas. Ask:

  • What do the colours tell us? (Green/light = lower elevation; browns/tans = higher elevation)
  • How can you tell where land is high or low? (Colour changes; contour lines)
  • Where is the land flat versus steep? (Point to examples: flat inland plains, steep Great Dividing Range)

Spend time on this. Show students where Sydney sits on the relief map. Ask them to trace the escarpment line (the sharp elevation change at the edge of the Blue Mountains). What does that steep line mean for settlement? This teaching needs to be thorough because the visual literacy will recur in L10 and L11.

Materials
  • Paper atlas – relief/topographic map one per student, NSW page
2

Map Comparison

Whole class

Compare Google Maps terrain view with the atlas relief map. Project the Google Maps terrain view of Sydney alongside (or switching with) the atlas relief map. Ask:

  • What does the terrain view show that the atlas relief map doesn’t? (Satellite imagery, buildings, streets, real-time features)
  • What does the relief map show that the terrain view doesn’t? (Elevation zones, landforms across a wider area, historical patterns)

Purpose: students understand that different map types emphasise different physical information.

Then show 2–3 early Sydney maps (from the State Library of NSW or similar) showing the early colony growing up around the harbour, coves, and freshwater sources. Ask:

  • How did the physical landscape shape where early settlers built? (Protected coves for landing, freshwater streams, flat land for clearing)
  • Do those settlement patterns still exist today? (Yes – Sydney’s CBD still hugs the harbour; residential areas still follow river valleys)

This makes the physical-geography-as-settlement-driver argument concrete and historical.

Materials
  • Google Maps terrain view projected, Sydney area pre-loaded
  • Early Sydney maps 2–3, teacher-sourced from State Library of NSW, projected
3

Paired Investigation

Pairs

Paired discussion: How did Sydney Harbour shape the city’s growth? Where did people settle first and why? Students must reason beyond surface observations. “Water access” is not sufficient. Discussion should unpack what the water offered:

  • Transport routes for moving goods in and out
  • Trade with visiting ships
  • Freshwater for sustenance (from nearby streams)
  • Sheltered anchorage for safe mooring
  • Defensive positions for the early colony

Pairs then annotate a physical features map of Sydney. The map shows coastline, harbour, major rivers, escarpment, and national park boundaries as unlabelled outlines. Pairs:

  1. Identify and label each major physical feature
  2. Write captions explaining the influence of each feature on settlement
  3. Ensure captions include reasoning, not just description

Example of surface description (not acceptable): “There is a river.” Example of reasoning (acceptable): “The Parramatta River provided freshwater and a transport route inland, which meant early settlers could sustain a colony and move goods.”

Extension

Pairs add a predictive layer – mark where they think future development might occur based on the physical geography. Ask: Why would development happen there? What physical features would attract it?

Support

Provide the map with major physical features already labelled (Sydney Harbour, Parramatta River, Georges River, Blue Mountains escarpment, national parks). Students focus on writing captions explaining influence on settlement using a frame: “[Feature name] influenced settlement because…”

  • Physical features outline map – Core A3, one per pair, printable template below
  • Physical features outline map – Support A3, pre-labelled version, printable template below
  • Paper atlas – relief/topographic one per pair for reference
  • Document camera for Stage 4 sharing
4

Sharing and Synthesis

Whole class

Selected pairs share their annotations under the document camera. As each pair presents, ask: Why did you write that caption? What reasoning is behind it? Listen for depth of explanation. Do they move beyond “water is good” to unpacking what water offers?

Class discussion: Which physical features had the biggest influence on where Sydney grew? Guide students toward the key conceptual takeaway:

Physical geography is not just background – it actively shaped human settlement decisions, and those early decisions continue to shape the city today.

Ask: Would Sydney have grown the same way without the harbour? Without the escarpment to the west? What does this tell us about the relationship between geography and human settlement?

Materials
  • Document camera for displaying annotated maps

Atlas Work

▾

Students read and interpret the relief/topographic map of NSW. This is the first time students systematically interpret elevation data from a map – the skill recurs in L10 (state scale) and L11 (national scale). During Stage 1, guide students to identify:

  • Elevation zones (low coastal plains, mid-elevation tablelands, high escarpment/mountains)
  • Major landform types (flat, rolling, steep)
  • Where Sydney sits in relation to the escarpment
  • How the physical landscape constrains or enables settlement

Pairs reference the atlas during Stage 3 to check physical features and understand the broader geography of the Sydney region.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Can students read contour lines and colour shading to interpret elevation from the relief map? Or do they struggle with the visual encoding?
  • In the paired discussion, do students go beyond “there was water” to explain what the water offered (transport, trade, freshwater, defence)?
  • In the annotated map captions, is the reasoning explicit? Do captions explain influence (e.g. “This feature allowed…”) or just describe (e.g. “There is a river”)?
  • Can students identify at least three major physical features and articulate how each shaped settlement?
  • In the sharing stage, can students defend their reasoning when asked?

How to capture

Circulate during Stages 1 and 3. Use a brief checklist to note which pairs understand relief map reading and which need re-teaching. During Stage 3, listen to the quality of paired discussion – note groups that unpack the why (transport, trade, freshwater, defence) versus surface observations. Collect all annotated maps as evidence. Photograph or scan the best examples to keep as models for future cohorts. During Stage 4, listen for the reasoning students use when explaining their captions – is the thinking sound?

What it tells you

This assessment reveals whether students have developed the visual literacy needed to read relief maps independently. If students struggle with Stage 1, they will need re-teaching before L10 and L11, which rely on relief map reading at scale. If settlement reasoning stays surface-level (just “water” or “shelter” without unpacking what these mean), the causal reasoning will need reinforcing when examining land use constraints in L8 and settlement patterns in global case studies (L13–14). Strong responses that articulate multiple influences (transport, trade, freshwater, defence) and can connect them to modern settlement patterns indicate readiness for the more complex geographical reasoning in Phase 3.

Printable Templates

Sydney Physical Features Maps

Teacher-sourced maps required for this lesson

The physical features maps for this lesson need to be sourced or created by the teacher, as auto-generated SVG maps do not produce print-quality geographic outlines.

Core Version – Unlabelled (A3, one per pair)

  • Sydney outline showing coastline, harbour, and major waterways
  • Physical features visible but unlabelled: Blue Mountains escarpment (western edge), national parks (Ku-ring-gai Chase, Royal), river systems (Parramatta, Georges)
  • Compass rose included
  • Space below or beside the map for four caption boxes where students explain how each feature influenced settlement
  • Pair names and date line at the top

Support Version – Pre-labelled (A3, 6–8 copies)

  • Same base map as core version
  • Major features pre-labelled: Sydney Harbour, Parramatta River, Georges River, Blue Mountains Escarpment, National Parks
  • Caption boxes include sentence starters: “Sydney Harbour influenced settlement because…”, “Parramatta River influenced settlement because…”, etc.

Suggested sources: Google Maps terrain view of Sydney printed to A3 (shows topography clearly), or a relief map from the school atlas photocopied to A3. A Google Maps view with terrain layer enabled gives a good sense of elevation without needing contour lines.
