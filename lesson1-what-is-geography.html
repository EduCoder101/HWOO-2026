Lesson 1 · What is Geography?

Map types, urban and rural places, and introduction to our local area
Lesson at a Glance
  • Part A – Maps, Places and Settlement Types: Revise that geographers use tools (relief maps, compass directions, grid maps, legends) to navigate and locate places
  • Compare physical and political world maps – what each shows, how they differ, and why different map types exist
  • Introduce urban, rural and remote settlement types with definitions; establish a unit vocabulary display
  • Use Google Maps (map view) to locate and classify NSW places: Newcastle (urban), Wagga Wagga (rural), Menindee (remote)
  • Students locate and classify additional places on the map using geographical tools
  • Part B – Our Local Area (if time allows): Google Maps zoom sequence from Roseville College street view to city-wide view
  • Categorise observations into human and physical features using a class T-chart
  • Six-map set at table groups – how different peoples represent the same place for different purposes
  • Mercator vs Gall-Peters projection provocation; student questions added to the vocabulary and question display

Prior Knowledge
None – this is the unit’s opening lesson. Students may have encountered compass directions, grid references and map symbols in earlier stages.
Enables
All subsequent lessons. Establishes physical/human distinction, map types, urban/rural/remote vocabulary, and the key provocation about representation.

Learning Intention

Understand that geography is the study of places, people and environments, and that geographers use different types of maps and tools to navigate and locate places.

Success Criteria

  • Identify tools geographers use to navigate and locate places
  • Explain the difference between physical and political maps
  • Define urban, rural and remote and classify places into these categories
  • Locate places on a map using geographical tools
  • Identify and categorise physical and human features in an image

Lesson Structure

Part A – Maps, Places and Settlement Types
1

Geographers’ Tools

Whole class

Introduce the idea that geographers use specific tools to navigate and locate places. Surface what students already know – some may recall tools from earlier years, others will be starting fresh. The purpose is activation, not assessment.

Key tools to name and briefly describe: relief maps (show the shape of the land – mountains, valleys, plains), compass directions (north, south, east, west and in-between), grid maps (use a grid to pinpoint locations), and legends (explain what symbols on a map mean). Students do not need to master these yet; they will be taught explicitly in subsequent lessons.

Materials
  • None required Discussion only
2

Physical and Political Maps

Whole class

Physical world map

Display a physical world map. Establish that physical maps show natural features of the Earth – mountain ranges, rivers, deserts, oceans, plains. Point out how colour is used to represent elevation and terrain.

Political world map

Display a political world map alongside (or after) the physical map. Establish that political maps show human features – country borders, capital cities, states and territories.

Comparing the two maps

Key questions:

  • What is similar between the two maps? (Each shows features; each is a world map.)
  • What is different between the two maps? (The types of features shown.)
  • Why is there more than one type of map? (Different map types show different information; different map types have different purposes.)

The core idea to land: maps are tools built for a purpose. The type of map you choose depends on what you need to find out.

Materials
  • Physical world map Projected on IWB or wall map. Teacher-sourced.
  • Political world map Projected on IWB or wall map. Teacher-sourced.
3

Urban, Rural and Remote

Whole class

Introduce three terms that describe the types of places people live in Australia. For each term, give the definition and ask students to suggest examples from their own experience before confirming.

  • Urban – places where many people live close together, such as towns and cities. These areas have lots of buildings, transport and services.
  • Rural – places that are outside towns and cities. They usually have more open space, farms and smaller communities.
  • Remote – places far from towns and cities, often harder to reach. Fewer people live there, and access to services such as schools or hospitals can take longer.

Add the three terms and definitions to the vocabulary display (butcher’s paper). Explain that this display will grow throughout the unit as new geographical vocabulary is introduced. Leave space below for student questions about places, which will be added later.

Support
For students unfamiliar with the concept, anchor each term to a concrete image: ‘Think of a city like Sydney – that’s urban. Think of a town surrounded by farms – that’s rural. Think of a place so far away it takes hours to drive to the nearest shop – that’s remote.’
Materials
  • Vocabulary display Butcher’s paper, prepared before lesson
4

Locating Places in NSW

Whole class, then individual

Worked examples

Open Google Maps in map view on the IWB, zoomed to show NSW. Locate three places as worked examples:

  • Newcastle (urban) – large city, dense streets, visible infrastructure
  • Wagga Wagga (rural) – smaller town, open land surrounding it, farms visible
  • Menindee (remote) – small settlement, vast distances to nearest towns, sparse features

For each place, model how to locate it: use the search function, then orient using natural and human features visible on the map around that location, and its proximity to other known places. Discuss what features on the map tell us whether a place is urban, rural or remote.

Student practice

Select individual students to locate a stated place on the projected map using geographical tools (search function, compass directions, nearby landmarks and features). After locating the place, the student classifies it as urban, rural or remote and explains their reasoning.

Suggested places: Broken Hill, Wollongong, Coffs Harbour, Tibooburra, Cobar. Choose a mix that gives students practice with each category.

Extension
After classifying a place, describe what geographical features on the map helped you decide. For example: ‘I can see the streets are spread apart and there is farmland around it, so I think it is rural.’
Support
Narrow the task: provide two options (‘Do you think this is urban or rural?’) rather than three. Point out specific features on the map to guide the student’s reasoning.
Materials
  • Google Maps on IWB Map view, zoomed to NSW
Part B – Our Local Area (if time allows)
5

Google Maps Zoom Sequence

Whole class

Transition from the NSW-wide view to a focus on the local area. Students have just been locating places across the state; now they zoom into a place they know well.

Project Google Maps on the IWB. Students note observations in their books throughout.

Street view of Roseville College

Students observe what they can see. Prompt them to notice small details: a stormwater drain, a street light – what does this imply about hidden infrastructure beneath the surface?

Aerial view of Roseville College and surrounds

Zoom out to show the train station, surrounding streets, and broader neighbourhood. Students write down all the features they observe (people, cars, trees, shade, sports courts, train station, shops, etc.).

City-wide view

Zoom out further. Individual features become largely indistinguishable, but the broader sense of urban density, green spaces, and transport corridors emerges. The scale has changed what is visible and what matters.

Connect back to Part A: Based on what we can see, would we classify Roseville as urban, rural or remote? What features tell us that?

Materials
  • Google Maps on IWB Switch to street view at Roseville College
6

Categorisation

Whole class, then individual

Ask students: in what ways might they organise all the things they have observed?

  • Promote discussion about human-built vs natural/physical features of the landscape
  • Push them to think about how they can subcategorise the human-built observations (transport, services/shops, infrastructure, housing vs business areas, recreational, parks)
  • Create a class T-chart of Human / Physical on the board, then students group their ideas in their books into categories that emerge from the discussion
Materials
  • None required Board work and student books
7

Map Set

Table groups

Six maps printed A4, clipped or stapled as a set. Students may write notes directly on the maps – print one set per student if individual annotation is preferred, or one per table for shared scribing.

The six maps:

  1. Current Sydney satellite image, no labels (Google Maps screenshot)
  2. Current Sydney street map with full labels and key (Google Maps screenshot)
  3. Sydney train network map (Transport for NSW – transportnsw.info, freely available PDF)
  4. Sydney topographic map (NSW Spatial Services – topo.maptiles.nsw.gov.au)
  5. Historical Sydney map – teacher-sourced. Two suggested eras: goldfields-era and early 20th century. Select maps that show a recognisably different city.
  6. Indigenous map of Cammeraygal Country – school-specific digital file (details to be supplied). This map sits alongside the others as an equal member of the set, not a supplementary resource.

Students observe the different types of maps. Key questions: How do these maps differ? What does this tell us about the purpose of maps?

Contrasting ways of representing and navigating place

Once students have discussed the first five maps, draw attention to the Cammeraygal Country map. Key questions:

  • What does this map show that the Google Maps view does not?
  • What does the Google Maps view show that this map does not?
  • If you wanted to understand what this place means to the people who have lived here for thousands of years, which map would you use? Why?
  • Are any of these maps the ‘right’ map, or do they each show something different about the same place?

The point is not that one map is better than another, but that different peoples represent the same place in fundamentally different ways depending on their relationship with it. The five modern maps represent the landscape, transport, elevation and history. The Cammeraygal map represents connection, Country and belonging. Both are ways of navigating and understanding place – neither is complete without the other.

Follow up: What does this tell us about how we can view our home, or other places on Earth? The aim is to elicit the concept that we can observe and represent places with a purpose (physical landscape, environment, political, social/demographic, historical, cultural, spiritual) – and that the choice of representation shapes what we see and what we value.

Students write down: ‘How we represent places impacts our decision making in how we manage them.’

Extension
Choose two maps from the set (including the Cammeraygal Country map as one option) and write three sentences explaining what each map is useful for and who might use it.
Materials
  • Six-map set A4, one set per table group
8

Projections Provocation and Student Questions

Whole class, then individual

Mercator and Gall-Peters projections

Two side-by-side images of the world projected on the whiteboard: Mercator projection and Gall-Peters projection. Source via Google Images – no printing required.

Key questions: What do you observe? What are the differences? Which is the right map?

Explain that the world map they are used to is not an accurate representation – the countries and continents are the wrong sizes, and the Gall-Peters projection is more accurate. Discuss the implications. Tie back to the key statement: ‘How we represent places impacts our decision making in how we manage them.’

Students write down: ‘Geography isn’t just about knowing where things are, it’s about asking why we represent the world the way we do.’

Student questions

Return to the vocabulary display (butcher’s paper) from Part A. Below the urban/rural/remote definitions, there is space for student questions. Post-it notes and pens at each table.

Students write down their questions about places, names attached. The display remains visible for the duration of the unit – position it where students can see it from their seats if possible. New vocabulary and questions are added throughout the unit.

Materials
  • Mercator and Gall-Peters projections Projected on IWB
  • Vocabulary display Continues from Part A
  • Post-it notes At each table group

Teaching Notes

▾

Part A as core content

Part A is the core teaching content for this lesson. Part B extends into the local area and should follow naturally if time allows. If the lesson runs as a single period rather than a double, Part A stands alone and Part B can be picked up at the start of Lesson 2.

Prior learning

The tools revision in Stage 1 (relief maps, compass directions, grid maps, legends) assumes no prior mastery. Some students may recall these from Stage 2 geography; others will encounter them for the first time. Frame the revision as light activation (‘you may have seen some of these before’) rather than a check for understanding. These tools are taught explicitly in Lessons 2 and 3.

Urban, rural and remote definitions

The definitions used in this lesson are drawn from the Australian Bureau of Statistics. ‘Urban’ and ‘rural’ are reasonably intuitive; ‘remote’ is the term students are least likely to have encountered. Anchor it concretely – distance from services, travel time, population density.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Physical vs political maps: Can students articulate the difference between what a physical map shows and what a political map shows? This is the foundational distinction for map literacy in the unit.
  • Urban, rural and remote classification: When asked to classify a place on Google Maps, can students use visible features to justify their choice? Note students who rely on the label alone vs those who reference what they see on the map.
  • Physical vs human features (Part B): Can students distinguish between physical and human features, or do they conflate them? This is the most important thing to note from Part B – if the distinction is not landing, build a recap into the start of L2.
  • Participation: Who contributes during whole-class discussions? Note quieter students who may need more structured entry points in coming lessons.

How to capture

Use the Phase 1 checklist. After the lesson, mark columns for ‘Distinguishes map types’ and ‘Classifies urban/rural/remote’ – ✓, ✓+, –, or ? – based on the Google Maps activity and class discussion.

What it tells you

If more than a third of the class is unclear on the physical/human distinction or cannot use map features to classify places, build a recap into the start of L2 before introducing compass directions and symbols.
