Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Learning Intention: Describe the geographical diversity of New South Wales

  • Name and locate NSW’s major geographical regions on a map
  • Describe how physical environments vary across NSW
  • Explain how location influences the way people live
Lesson Structure
1

NSW – A State of Regions

Whole class

Introduce the scale shift from city to state. Use Google Maps to zoom out from Sydney to NSW, drawing attention to how the landscape changes: the coastal strip gives way to mountains, then flatter country, then vast arid plains. The purpose is to make the sheer size and diversity of NSW visually concrete before students open the atlas.

Introduce NSW’s five broad regions: the coastal strip (where most people live, including Sydney), the Great Dividing Range (mountains running north–south), the western slopes (fertile farming country on the inland side of the range), the western plains (flat, dry, pastoral land), and the outback (arid, sparsely populated). Introduce the vocabulary term settlement – a place where people have established a community, from a small town to a major city.

Aboriginal Land Management

Before students begin their investigation, provide context that the landscapes they are about to study have been actively managed by Aboriginal peoples for tens of thousands of years. Distribute the Aboriginal Fire Management information text (one per group). Students read the text, which explains how cultural burning – deliberate, controlled fire applied in mosaic patterns across the landscape – shaped vegetation, maintained biodiversity, and created the open woodland and grassland environments that European settlers later encountered and described as ‘natural’.

Brief class discussion after reading: What does this tell us about the idea of ‘natural’ landscapes? How does knowing this change the way we think about the regions we’re about to investigate?

This is not a passing mention. The concept that Country has been cared for and managed through sophisticated fire practices is foundational to understanding NSW’s landscapes.

Materials
  • Google Maps projected on IWB
  • Paper atlas NSW maps, one per student
  • Aboriginal Fire Management text one per group, printable template below
2

Regional Investigation

Small groups

Each group is assigned one of the five NSW regions: coastal strip, Great Dividing Range, western slopes, western plains, or outback. Using the atlas (physical and political maps of NSW), groups investigate their region and record findings on the structured template.

Investigation Prompts

The template guides students through four areas of investigation:

  • Physical Features: What landforms, waterways, and natural features define this region? Use the physical map to identify mountains, rivers, plains, coastline, or desert.
  • Climate and Vegetation: What is the climate like? What vegetation can you infer from the physical geography? (Students may cross–reference the climate/vegetation maps if available in their atlas.)
  • Settlements and Towns: What towns and cities exist in this region? Are they clustered or spread out? Use the political map to find major settlements.
  • How People Use the Land: Based on what you can see in the atlas and what you know about the physical environment, what do people do here? Farming? Mining? Tourism? Services?

At the bottom of the template, groups address the question: ‘Why do people live in this region?’ This connects the investigation back to the pull factors concept from L5.

Extension

Groups add a ‘connections’ layer: how does their region connect to others? Think about freight routes, water supply, tourism flows, road and rail links. They note these connections to share during Stage 3.

Support

Provide a partially completed template with physical features pre–identified for the assigned region. Students focus on settlement patterns and land use rather than identifying the features themselves.

Materials
  • Paper atlas NSW physical and political maps, one per student
  • Regional Investigation Template one per student, printable template below
3

Mapping and Sharing

Whole class

Groups present a brief summary of their region to the class. As each group presents, the class collaboratively annotates the large blank NSW map, building up a picture of the state’s geographical diversity. Each group adds their region’s key features, settlements, and land use patterns to the map.

While the class map is being built, each student also annotates their own individual A4 NSW outline map with the information shared by all groups. This ensures individual accountability – every student leaves with a complete annotated map, not just the group that investigated their region.

Discussion

Once all regions are mapped, draw the lesson together with two questions:

  • How does where you live shape how you live? (Connect back to L5 pull factors and L8 land use.)
  • What systems connect these regions to each other? (Transport, supply chains, communication, water. This seeds the ‘systems’ thinking that continues through Phase 3.)

Students use the NSW physical and political atlas maps as their primary investigation tool. This is the first lesson where atlas work is the core activity rather than a supplement to screen–based teaching. Groups navigate the atlas independently to find physical features, settlements, and land use evidence for their assigned region. Individual students then use the atlas to verify and extend what they hear from other groups during the sharing stage.

Key atlas skills in use: reading physical features from a topographic/relief map (L7), interpreting settlement patterns from a political map, and connecting physical geography to human activity. All skills from Phase 1–2 are assumed.

What to Look For

  • Can students name and locate their assigned region on the atlas map?
  • Do they accurately describe the physical environment using geographical vocabulary (e.g. ‘the Great Dividing Range runs north to south’, not ‘there are mountains’)?
  • Can they connect physical features to settlement patterns and land use?
  • During the sharing stage, are students annotating their individual maps with information from other groups?

How to Capture

Circulate during Stage 2 (group investigation) with the Phase 3 observation checklist. Note the quality of regional description during group discussions and presentations. Check individual annotated maps at the end of Stage 3 – these are the assessment artefact.

What It Tells You

Students who struggle to read the atlas independently may need additional support in L11 (which uses the same atlas skills at national scale). Students who describe regions in generic terms (‘it’s hot and dry’) rather than with specific geographical vocabulary may need vocabulary support before the L12 written comparison.

Aboriginal Fire Management: Caring for Country

For tens of thousands of years, Aboriginal peoples across Australia have used fire as a tool for managing the land. This practice, known as cultural burning or fire–stick farming, involves lighting small, controlled fires at specific times of the year.

Cultural burning serves many purposes. Low, cool fires clear away dead leaves, bark, and dry grass from the forest floor. This reduces the fuel that could feed dangerous bushfires. The fires also encourage new plant growth, which attracts animals for hunting. Different plants respond to fire in different ways – some need fire to release their seeds, while others grow back quickly after burning, providing fresh food for people and wildlife.

Aboriginal fire managers read the land carefully. They observe which areas need burning and which should be left alone. They burn in a mosaic pattern – small patches at different times – so that animals always have unburnt areas to shelter in. This creates a patchwork of different vegetation types across the landscape.

When European settlers arrived in Australia, they described the land as ‘natural’ bush and parkland. But what they were actually seeing were landscapes that had been carefully managed through fire for thousands of generations. The open woodlands, grassy plains, and diverse forests of NSW were not wild or untouched – they were the result of sophisticated land management.

Today, many communities and fire services are working with Aboriginal fire managers to reintroduce cultural burning practices. Scientists have found that these traditional methods can reduce bushfire risk, improve biodiversity, and help landscapes recover from damage.

Key terms: cultural burning · fire–stick farming · mosaic pattern · biodiversity · land management

Discussion: What does this tell us about the idea of ‘natural’ landscapes? If Aboriginal peoples have been managing the land for tens of thousands of years, can we call any part of Australia truly ‘natural’?
