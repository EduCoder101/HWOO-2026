Lesson 11 · Australia: Physical Geography

A four-station atlas investigation of Australia’s landforms, climate, vegetation, and population

At a Glance

In this lesson, students rotate through four atlas stations, each focused on a different thematic map of Australia. At each station, they examine the map closely and record key observations on a structured template.

Station 1 – Physical Features map: major landforms, river systems, coastal features

Station 2 – Climate Zones map: tropical, arid/semi-arid, temperate zones and their patterns

Station 3 – Vegetation map: rainforest, woodland, grassland, desert scrub and connections to climate

Station 4 – Population Distribution map: where people live, where they don’t, and why

At each station, students record key observations on their template. During the whole-class synthesis, students discover what patterns emerge across all four maps. The class collaboratively builds a large Australia map combining findings from all four stations.

Key vocabulary: population distribution

Learning Intention: Describe Australia’s major physical features and environmental regions

  • Name and locate Australia’s major landforms, river systems and environmental regions
  • Read and interpret a thematic map of Australia (climate or vegetation)
  • Explain what makes Australia’s physical geography distinctive
Lesson Structure

Stage 1: Station Rotation Setup

Whole class

Introduce the task: students will rotate through four atlas stations, each focusing on a different thematic map of Australia. At each station, they examine the map closely and record key observations on their template.

Explain the rotation system: groups move to the next station when signalled. At each station, students work together to read the map, discuss what they notice, and record their own observations individually on their template.

Distribute the Station Rotation Template (one per student). Draw attention to the four quadrants – each corresponds to a station. Students fill in the relevant quadrant at each station.

Preview of Each Station

  • Station 1 – Physical Features: the physical map showing landforms, mountains, rivers, and coastline
  • Station 2 – Climate Zones: the climate map showing Australia’s different climate regions
  • Station 3 – Vegetation: the vegetation map showing what grows where
  • Station 4 – Population Distribution: the population map showing where people live

The station labels at each table include investigation prompts to guide students’ observations. Students do not need to answer every prompt – the prompts help focus their looking.

Materials
  • Station labels one per station, printable template below
  • Station Rotation Template one per student, printable template below

Stage 2: Four-Station Atlas Investigation

Small groups rotating

Students rotate through all four stations. The teacher signals when to rotate.

Station 1 – Physical Features

Atlas map: Australia physical map. Students identify and locate major features:

  • The Great Dividing Range – how far does it stretch? How close to the coast is it?
  • Major river systems – the Murray-Darling Basin, the rivers of the Top End
  • Desert regions – the Simpson Desert, Great Sandy Desert, Gibson Desert, Great Victoria Desert
  • Coastal features – the Great Barrier Reef, Gulf of Carpentaria, Bass Strait, the Nullarbor cliffs
  • Uluru/Kata Tjuta and the MacDonnell Ranges in central Australia
  • Lake Eyre – the lowest point on the continent, usually dry

Investigation prompts: What is the dominant physical feature of Australia’s interior? How does the east coast differ from the west? Where are the highest points?

Station 2 – Climate Zones

Atlas map: Australia climate map. Students identify the major climate zones:

  • Tropical (northern Australia) – hot and wet, with a distinct wet and dry season
  • Arid and semi-arid (most of the interior) – very low rainfall, extreme temperatures
  • Temperate (southern coastal fringe) – moderate rainfall, milder temperatures
  • Subtropical (parts of the east coast) – warm with reliable rainfall

Investigation prompts: Which climate zone covers the largest area? Where does the most rain fall? What is the relationship between latitude and climate in Australia? How might climate affect where people choose to live?

Station 3 – Vegetation

Atlas map: Australia vegetation map. Students identify major vegetation types:

  • Tropical rainforest (far north Queensland, small pockets)
  • Open woodland and eucalyptus forest (eastern and northern Australia)
  • Grassland and savanna (northern tropical regions)
  • Scrubland and mallee (semi-arid transition zones)
  • Desert scrub and spinifex (arid interior)
  • Alpine vegetation (small areas of the Snowy Mountains)

Investigation prompts: How does vegetation change as you move from the coast inland? Can you see a connection between the vegetation map and the climate map from Station 2? Where would you expect the most diverse vegetation? Why do you think eucalyptus dominates so much of Australia?

Station 4 – Population Distribution

Atlas map: Australia population distribution map. Students identify patterns:

  • The coastal concentration: most Australians live within 50km of the coast
  • The five major capital city clusters (Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Perth, Adelaide)
  • The vast, sparsely populated interior
  • Smaller regional centres and their distribution
  • The northern coastline – tropical but still relatively unpopulated

Investigation prompts: Where do most people live? Where do very few people live? Can you explain the pattern using what you learned at the other stations? Why might the tropical north have fewer people than the temperate south-east? What does this map tell you about Australia that the physical map alone doesn’t?

Extension

At each station, students add a ‘connection’ observation: how does what they see on this map relate to what they recorded at a previous station? For example, at Station 3 (vegetation), they might note how vegetation patterns mirror climate zones from Station 2.

Support

Provide a template with guiding prompts that focus observation more tightly. For example, at Station 1: ‘Find the Great Dividing Range. What side of it do most cities sit on?’ At Station 4: ‘Count the number of cities you can find in the interior of Australia. Now count the cities on the coast.’

Materials
  • Paper atlas Australia maps, multiple copies per station
  • Station labels at each station

Stage 3: Synthesis

Whole class

Bring the class together to connect findings across all four stations. This is where the four separate investigations become one understanding of Australia’s geography.

Key Questions for Discussion

  • What patterns emerge when you compare the four maps? (Climate zones mirror vegetation types; population mirrors both; the Great Dividing Range is a dividing line for climate, vegetation, and settlement.)
  • Why is most of Australia sparsely populated? (Arid interior, extreme temperatures, limited water, distance from services.)
  • Where would you expect the biggest cities to be, based on the physical geography and climate? (Temperate coastal areas with harbour/river access – which matches the actual pattern.)
  • What makes Australia’s physical geography distinctive compared to other continents? (The vast arid interior, the age and flatness of the landscape, the Great Barrier Reef, the dominance of eucalyptus.)

Class Collaboration

As the discussion progresses, collaboratively build the class Australia map. Groups contribute information from their station observations: physical features from Station 1 groups, climate zone boundaries from Station 2, vegetation labels from Station 3, and population centres from Station 4.

Students complete the synthesis prompt on their template: ‘What patterns did you notice across all four maps?’

Introduce the vocabulary term population distribution formally: the way people are spread across a geographic area. This concept becomes the analytical focus in L12.

This is the most atlas-intensive lesson in the unit. Students work with four different thematic maps of Australia across the station rotation. The atlas is the sole investigation tool – no screen resources are used. Students need to read physical features maps (building on L7 topography skills), interpret climate and vegetation maps (new skill for this lesson), and analyse population distribution maps. The key progression from L10 is that students now work with multiple map types for the same area, comparing and connecting them to build a layered understanding.

What to Look For

  • Can students read and interpret each thematic map type (physical, climate, vegetation, population)?
  • Are observations specific and geographic (e.g. ‘the arid zone covers most of the interior west of the Great Dividing Range’) rather than generic (‘it’s dry in the middle’)?
  • Can students make connections across maps during the synthesis discussion?
  • Are individual templates completed with substantive observations at each station?

How to Capture

Circulate during Stage 2 (station rotation) with the Phase 3 observation checklist. Listen to group discussions at each station. Check template quality at end of Stage 2 – look for specificity and use of geographical vocabulary. Note students who make cross-map connections during synthesis (Stage 3) – this indicates strong geographical thinking.

What It Tells You

Students who struggle with thematic map reading may need additional atlas orientation before L12. Students who make strong cross-map connections are ready for the more independent investigation in L12. The population distribution observations from Station 4 directly preview L12’s content – note which students already have hypotheses about why the coast is more populated.
