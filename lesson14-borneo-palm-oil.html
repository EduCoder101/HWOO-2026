Lesson 14 · Human Impact: Borneo Palm Oil

How palm oil farming has transformed a tropical landscape, and why it matters to us
Lesson at a Glance
  • Students locate Borneo on the world atlas and examine satellite imagery showing the scale of deforestation for palm oil plantations
  • Individual investigation using four teacher-curated fact cards covering palm oil in everyday products, deforestation, consequences, and sustainability efforts
  • Before–after satellite images make the transformation visible and immediate
  • Class discussion explores personal connection: how are we linked to what is happening in Borneo?
  • Individual cross–case comparison: students write a structured geographical explanation comparing Murray–Darling (L13) and Borneo using a graphic organiser scaffold

Learning Intention

Explain how farming for palm oil has transformed Borneo’s environment and consider our connection to this change

Success Criteria

  • Locate Borneo on a world map and describe its physical environment
  • Explain how palm oil farming has changed Borneo’s landscape
  • Identify at least two consequences for communities and ecosystems
  • Describe a connection between their own lives and the changes in Borneo

Lesson Structure

1

Locating Borneo

Whole class

Students locate Borneo on the world atlas and Southeast Asia regional map. Note its equatorial location, its position relative to Australia (roughly north–northwest, approximately 4,000 km), and its physical geography: tropical rainforest, major river systems, exceptional biodiversity.

Google Maps satellite view is projected on the IWB. The teacher zooms from world view to Borneo. The deep green of intact rainforest is visually striking. Then zoom to a specific area showing the geometric patchwork of palm oil plantations cutting into the forest. The contrast is immediate and powerful.

Before–after satellite images displayed side by side. Students observe and describe the change before any explanation is provided. Key question: What has happened to this landscape, and what might have caused it?

This is the first time students locate a place in Southeast Asia on the atlas. They have used the world atlas (L3 place cards) and Australia maps extensively, but navigating to a specific island in the Asian regional map is a new application of their atlas skills.

Materials
  • Paper atlas world map, Southeast Asia regional map (class set)
  • Google Maps satellite view, IWB
  • Before–after satellite images projected or A3
2

Fact Card Investigation

Individual

Individual investigation using teacher-curated fact cards. Each student receives a set of four A5 laminated cards:

Card 1 – “Palm Oil is Everywhere”

What palm oil is and where it appears in everyday products: shampoo, chocolate, biscuits, margarine, instant noodles, ice cream, lipstick. Why it is so widely used (cheap, versatile, high yield per hectare). Global production scale.

Card 2 – “Disappearing Forest”

The scale and speed of deforestation. Borneo has lost more than half its rainforest since the 1970s. Satellite evidence of the transformation. How plantations replace complex rainforest with monoculture rows. The role of international demand in driving clearing.

Card 3 – “The Cost of Clearing”

Consequences for wildlife (orangutans, pygmy elephants, proboscis monkeys losing habitat), local communities (displacement of Indigenous Dayak peoples, loss of traditional forest livelihoods), and global climate (carbon release from peat fires, reduced carbon absorption).

Card 4 – “Can Palm Oil Be Sustainable?”

Efforts to establish sustainable palm oil certification (RSPO). What “sustainable” means in this context. Challenges: enforcement, cost, smallholder farmers. What consumers can do. The tension between conservation and economic development for producing countries.

Students read all four cards and record key findings. Unlike L13 (where pairs split cards), individual work means every student engages with all content. This is appropriate for the shift toward independent inquiry.

Extension

Students evaluate which case study (Murray–Darling or Borneo) has more severe consequences and justify their reasoning, weighing evidence from both investigations.

Support

Students receive a structured note-taking sheet with headings matching the four card topics and sentence starters under each.

Materials
  • Fact card sets 4× A5 laminated cards per student (printable template below)
  • Note-taking sheet support resource (printable template below)
3

Discussion and Cross-Case Comparison

Whole class then individual

Class discussion bridges from the Borneo investigation to the cross-case comparison. Key questions:

  • How are we connected to what is happening in Borneo?
  • What does responsibility look like when the impact is on the other side of the world?

The teacher explicitly connects to the Murray–Darling case from L13: both cases involve farming changing an environment; both have consequences for ecosystems and communities. But the scale, distance, and our personal connection differ.

Individual cross-case comparison using a structured graphic organiser. Students complete the organiser with four sections:

  • “What changed?” – describe the environmental transformation in each case
  • “Why did it change?” – explain the human decisions that caused the change
  • “Who was affected?” – identify the communities and ecosystems impacted
  • “Our connection” – describe how their own lives relate to each case

This comparison is the core assessment for the human impact pair of lessons. It is collected.

Support

The graphic organiser includes pre-populated prompts and sentence starters within each section. Students receive the sentence starters sheet for additional scaffolding.

Materials
  • Comparison graphic organiser A4 (printable template below)
  • Sentence starters support resource (printable template below)

Atlas Work

▾

Locating Borneo

Locate Borneo on the world atlas and Southeast Asia regional map. Note its position relative to Australia (roughly north–northwest, approximately 4,000 km). Identify the equatorial location and note the climate implications (tropical, high rainfall). Students should observe that Borneo is shared by three countries: Malaysia, Indonesia, and Brunei.

This is the first Southeast Asia location work students have done. Previous atlas work has focused on Australia and the local region. Navigating from the world map to a specific island in Asia is a new scale of atlas skill.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to Look For

  • Can students locate Borneo independently on the atlas?
  • Do students identify specific consequences from the fact cards, not just general statements?
  • Does the comparison include both similarities and differences between Murray–Darling and Borneo?
  • Does the writing reference the personal connection (palm oil in everyday products)?

How to Capture

The written comparison graphic organiser is collected. This is the main assessment data for the human impact pair of lessons. Phase 3 observation checklist for discussion quality.

What It Tells You

Students who can describe each case but cannot draw meaningful comparisons will need more scaffolding in L15’s megacity comparison. Look specifically at the “Our connection” section – students who struggle here may find it difficult to engage with the global perspective required in Phases 4–5.

Printable Templates

Fact Cards: Borneo Palm Oil

Palm Oil is Everywhere
  • Palm oil comes from the fruit of oil palm trees, grown in tropical regions near the equator
  • It is found in about half of all supermarket products: shampoo, soap, chocolate, biscuits, margarine, ice cream, instant noodles, lipstick
  • Oil palm produces more oil per hectare than any other oil crop, making it cheap and efficient
  • Global production has doubled in the last 20 years, driven by rising demand
  • Indonesia and Malaysia (including Borneo) produce about 85% of the world’s palm oil
[Teacher: insert image]
Check the ingredients of three products in your home tonight. How many contain palm oil?
Disappearing Forest
  • Borneo’s tropical rainforest is one of the oldest in the world – over 130 million years old
  • More than half of Borneo’s rainforest has been cleared since the 1970s
  • Most clearing is for palm oil plantations: rows of identical trees replacing complex, biodiverse forest
  • Satellite images show the dramatic scale of change – vast areas of green replaced by geometric plantation grids
  • International demand for cheap palm oil drives the clearing; companies and governments profit from the exports
[Teacher: insert image]
If the demand is coming from countries like Australia, who is really responsible for the clearing?
The Cost of Clearing
  • Orangutans have lost 80% of their habitat; they are now critically endangered
  • Borneo pygmy elephants and proboscis monkeys are also threatened by habitat loss
  • Indigenous Dayak communities have been displaced from their traditional forest lands
  • Peat forests, when drained and burned for plantations, release enormous amounts of carbon dioxide
  • In 2015, peat fires in Indonesia caused a haze emergency across Southeast Asia, affecting millions of people
[Teacher: insert image]
Is it fair that communities and wildlife in Borneo pay the price for products used around the world?
Can Palm Oil Be Sustainable?
  • The Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil (RSPO) certifies plantations that meet environmental and social standards
  • “Sustainable” means no new deforestation, no planting on peat, fair treatment of workers, and respect for community land rights
  • Only about 19% of global palm oil is RSPO certified
  • Some argue that boycotting palm oil would make things worse – other oil crops need even more land
  • Consumers can look for the RSPO label, and companies can commit to deforestation–free supply chains
[Teacher: insert image]
Is buying “sustainable” palm oil enough, or do we need to change how much we use?
