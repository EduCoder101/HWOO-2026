Phase 4 · Global

Lesson 15 · Megacities: Mumbai and Sydney

Comparing two global cities to understand what makes a megacity work

Lesson at a Glance

  • Introduction to the megacity concept: definition, global examples located on the world atlas, and patterns in where megacities are found
  • Pairs investigate Mumbai and Sydney using Google Maps and six teacher-curated fact cards covering population, physical setting, land use, services, challenges, and transport
  • Individual comparison graphic organiser identifying at least three similarities and three differences
  • Class discussion: what challenges do megacities face, what systems allow 20 million people to live together, and could Sydney become one?

Learning Intention

Compare the human and physical geography of two contrasting global cities

Success Criteria

  • Define ‘megacity’ and name at least three examples from different continents
  • Identify at least three similarities and three differences between Mumbai and Sydney
  • Use geographical vocabulary accurately in a comparison graphic organiser

Lesson Structure

1

What is a Megacity?

Whole class

Introduce the concept of megacity: a city with a population of 10 million or more. This is new vocabulary for the unit.

A list of the world’s megacities is displayed: Tokyo, Delhi, Shanghai, Mumbai, São Paulo, Mexico City, Cairo, Beijing, Dhaka, Osaka, Lagos, Istanbul. Students locate five or six of these on the world atlas and note patterns: most are in Asia; most are coastal or situated on major rivers; none are in Australia, and very few are in Europe.

Key question: Sydney has about 5.5 million people. Mumbai has about 21 million. What would need to change for Sydney to become a megacity?

This question plants a seed for the comparison investigation and connects back to the population distribution work from L11–12.

Materials
  • Paper atlas world map and regional Asia map
  • Megacity list projected or printed, 12 cities
2

Mumbai and Sydney Investigation

Pairs

Pairs investigate both cities using Google Maps and teacher-curated fact cards. Each pair receives six A5 laminated cards: three for Mumbai and three for Sydney.

Pairs use Google Maps alongside the fact cards, zooming into areas mentioned on the cards (Dharavi, Marine Drive, the Harbour Bridge area, Western Sydney suburbs) to see what these places look like.

ACH-01 Integration

The Sydney fact card includes the Indigenous Country acknowledgement. During investigation, pairs should note how Indigenous place names and Country are (or are not) represented in the way Sydney is mapped and described on Google Maps. This is substantive content, not a footnote.

Extension

Students add a ‘What could Sydney learn from Mumbai?’ section to their graphic organiser, requiring evaluative thinking rather than just comparison.

Support

Pre-populated graphic organiser with category headings and one example similarity and one example difference already filled in as models.

Materials
  • Fact card sets 6 cards per pair, printable template below
  • Google Maps student devices or IWB, pre-loaded
  • Paper atlas world and Asia regional maps
3

Graphic Organiser and Discussion

Individual then whole class

Individual: Comparison Graphic Organiser

Students complete a structured comparison graphic organiser identifying at least three similarities and three differences between Mumbai and Sydney. The organiser uses category headings: Physical Geography, Population, Services, Challenges, Transport.

Under each category, students write one or more points for each city, then identify whether it represents a similarity or difference.

Class Discussion

Class discussion draws out systems thinking. Key questions:

  • What challenges do megacities face that smaller cities do not?
  • What systems allow a city of 20 million people to function?
  • Could Sydney become a megacity? What would need to change?

The discussion should surface transport, water, food supply, waste management, and housing as the ‘systems’ that sustain large urban populations. Students have encountered systems language in L8 (land use interdependence) and L13–14 (water/farming systems).

Materials
  • Comparison graphic organiser one per student, printable template below

Reference

Materials & Resources

Physical Materials

  • Paper atlas: World map and Asia regional map (class set)
  • Fact card sets: 6 × A5 laminated cards per pair – 3 Mumbai + 3 Sydney (see printable template)
  • Comparison graphic organiser: A4 structured sheet with five category rows (see printable template)

Digital Resources

  • Google Maps: Pre-load Mumbai and Sydney. Useful zoom locations: Dharavi, Marine Drive, Chhatrapati Shivaji Terminus (Mumbai); Circular Quay, Parramatta, Western Sydney (Sydney)
  • Megacity list: 12 cities for projection or whiteboard display (Tokyo, Delhi, Shanghai, Mumbai, São Paulo, Mexico City, Cairo, Beijing, Dhaka, Osaka, Lagos, Istanbul)

Atlas Work

Locate Mumbai and Sydney on the world atlas. Use the Asia regional map for Mumbai’s physical context – note the west coast location, the Arabian Sea, and the Western Ghats mountain range inland. For Sydney, students can reference their existing knowledge from L6–8. Students should navigate to these locations independently; teacher support only if needed.

Observational Assessment

What to look for

  • Can students locate Mumbai on the atlas independently?
  • Do pairs use the fact cards to find specific evidence, or just skim the surface?
  • Does the graphic organiser distinguish genuine similarities from superficial ones (‘both have buildings’ vs ‘both are coastal cities built around harbours’)?
  • Does the discussion show systems thinking (transport, water, housing as interconnected systems)?

How to capture

Graphic organiser is collected. Note students who produce only surface-level comparisons – they may need more structured scaffolding in the World Heritage investigation.

What it tells you

This is the last lesson before the five-lesson World Heritage arc. Students who can identify meaningful similarities and differences with evidence are ready for independent research. Students who struggle with the comparison task will need additional support structures in L16.

Megacity Fact Cards (6 × A5, 3 pages – 2 per page)

Mumbai: Physical Setting & Population
  • Located on India’s west coast, on the shores of the Arabian Sea
  • Originally seven separate islands, connected through land reclamation over centuries
  • Population: approximately 21 million people in the metropolitan area
  • One of the most densely populated cities in the world – about 32,000 people per square kilometre in some areas
  • Monsoon climate: extremely heavy rainfall from June to September, with dry conditions the rest of the year

Mumbai: Land Use & Services
  • India’s financial capital – home to the Bombay Stock Exchange and the Reserve Bank of India
  • Centre of the Bollywood film industry, producing over 1,000 films per year
  • Mix of gleaming high-rise towers and informal settlements, sometimes side by side
  • Dharavi, one of Asia’s largest informal settlements, is home to over 600,000 people and has a thriving economy including recycling, pottery, and textiles
  • Public hospitals and schools serve millions, but are unevenly distributed across the city
[Teacher: insert image]
Mumbai: Challenges & Transport
  • Monsoon flooding regularly submerges low-lying areas, disrupting transport and damaging homes
  • Severe housing shortages: about 40% of Mumbai’s population lives in informal settlements
  • Air quality is a significant concern, especially during the dry season
  • The Mumbai suburban railway carries over 7 million passengers every day – one of the busiest rail systems in the world
  • Major infrastructure projects including a new Metro system aim to reduce congestion and improve connectivity
[Teacher: insert image]
Sydney: Physical Setting & Population
  • Located on Australia’s southeast coast, built around one of the world’s great natural harbours
  • Population: approximately 5.5 million people spread over a large metropolitan area
  • Much lower population density than Mumbai – about 400 people per square kilometre overall
  • Temperate climate with warm summers and mild winters; rainfall spread throughout the year
  • Built on the Country of the Gadigal, Wangal, Cammeraygal, and many other First Nations clan groups whose connection to this place extends back tens of thousands of years
[Teacher: insert image]
Sydney: Land Use & Services
  • The CBD is centred on the harbour, with distinct residential, commercial, and industrial zones spreading outward
  • Well-developed public services: hospitals, schools, and libraries distributed across suburbs
  • Significant recreational land use including the harbour, ocean beaches, and a network of national parks on the city fringe
  • Housing ranges from dense inner-city apartments to sprawling suburban homes in the outer west
  • Indigenous place names are preserved in some areas (Parramatta from Burramatta, ‘place where eels lie down’) but are largely absent from many Sydney maps
[Teacher: insert image]
Sydney: Challenges & Transport
  • Urban sprawl means long commute times, especially from the western suburbs to the CBD
  • Housing affordability is a major challenge – Sydney is one of the most expensive cities in the world to buy a home
  • Bushfire risk on the urban fringe, as the city expands into bushland
  • An extensive network of trains, buses, ferries, and light rail serves the metropolitan area
  • Major infrastructure projects (WestConnex motorway, Sydney Metro) aim to improve connectivity across the growing city
