Learning Intention

Investigate a World Heritage Site and produce an information report on its location, significance, management and threats

Success Criteria

  • Locate the chosen World Heritage Site on a map and describe its geographical context
  • Explain the cultural and/or natural significance of the site
  • Describe the management practices that protect the site
  • Identify and explain the threats the site faces
  • Produce a clearly structured information report using geographical vocabulary

Lesson Structure

1

Research Setup

Whole class

Brief orientation to today’s research focus. Students have selected their site and made initial observations in L16. Today they investigate two questions: where exactly is your site and what is its geographical context, and why is it significant?

Display the research framework on the board so students understand the recording structure. The context section asks for: physical environment (landforms, water features, vegetation), location relative to settlements and transport links, and climate. The significance section asks for: what makes this site important (cultural value, natural value, or both), and what specific features or qualities earned it World Heritage listing.

Distribute the L17 research templates and curated resource packs (or direct students to their library resources from L16). Remind students that the atlas and Google Maps are tools alongside the written sources, not replacements for them.

Materials
  • L17 Research Template printable template below, one per student
  • Curated resource packs or library books from L16 site selection
  • Paper atlas world map and relevant regional maps
2

Guided Research: Context and Significance

Individual

Extended individual research period. Students work through their curated resource pack and library books, using the atlas and Google Maps alongside the written sources. The research template guides their note-taking through two sections.

Geographical Context

Students record: the physical environment surrounding the site (at least three features), the site’s location relative to the nearest city or major settlement, transport links and accessibility, and the climate zone (using the atlas climate map).

Significance

Students record: whether the site is cultural, natural, or mixed heritage; the specific qualities that earned it World Heritage listing; and at least two reasons the site matters to humanity (not just to the country it is in).

Teacher conferencing

Teacher circulates and conferences throughout. Priority conferencing targets: students who struggled with site selection or atlas navigation in L16, and students whose initial observations from L16 were surface-level. Key conferencing question: “Can you show me on the atlas where your site is? What can you see around it?”

Mid-lesson check-in

After approximately 20–25 minutes, pause the class briefly. Two or three students share one finding about their site’s significance. This models the depth of thinking expected and gives the teacher a pulse check on research progress. Students then return to their research for the remainder of the lesson.

Extension

Students investigating two sites begin noting initial points of comparison during their research. Where do the two sites differ in physical context? Do they share any aspects of significance?

Support

The research template includes prompt questions to guide investigation (e.g. “What physical features are near the site?” “Is it in a city or a remote area?”). Teacher conferences prioritise support students in the first half of the research period.

Reference

Materials & Resources

▾
  • Paper atlas: World map and relevant regional maps for each of the 10 sites (class set)
  • Google Maps: Available on student devices or IWB for satellite and street view investigation
  • Curated resource packs or library books: One set per student, matched to their chosen site. Each set should include: a one-page overview (from L16), 2–3 source documents or extracts at Year 5 reading level, relevant map references, and 3–4 photographs. Book library resources wherever possible; supplement with curated online resources as needed.
  • L17 Research Template: A4 structured sheet with two sections (context and significance). See printable template below.

Atlas Work

▾

Students use the atlas throughout this lesson as an integrated research tool. During the context investigation, they identify physical features surrounding the site, note nearby cities and transport routes, and check the climate zone using the atlas climate map. Atlas navigation should be independent at this point – students have been building these skills since L1.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Can students locate and describe the physical environment surrounding their site using specific geographical language?
  • Are students recording context observations that go beyond surface description (not just “it is near mountains” but “the site is in a tropical rainforest on the coast, surrounded by mountains to the west”)?
  • Can students articulate why their site is significant in terms that go beyond “it is famous” or “it is old”?
  • Are extension students beginning to note comparison points between their two sites?

How to capture

Research templates are the primary artefact. Teacher notes which students needed conferencing support and what the support focused on (atlas navigation, depth of observation, or significance reasoning). These notes inform conferencing priorities in L18.

What it tells you

Students who produce detailed context observations and clear significance statements are on track for the report. Students whose context notes are thin or whose significance reasoning is vague will need targeted conferencing in L18 before they move to management and threats. Extension students who are not yet noting comparison points may need prompting at the start of L18.
