Lesson 20 · World Heritage: Revision and Completion

Refining your report and completing the investigation
Lesson at a Glance
  • Brief whole-class revision focus: students identify one specific revision from their L19 peer feedback
  • Extended individual revision and completion period with teacher conferencing
  • Self-assessment against success criteria before submission
  • Reports collected; brief whole-class reflection linking the investigation to the unit arc and foreshadowing the summative task

Enables
L21–24 (Design a Place summative task). The information report quality is diagnostic for the written explanation component of the summative task.

Learning Intention

Investigate a World Heritage Site and produce an information report on its location, significance, management and threats

Success Criteria

  • Locate the chosen World Heritage Site on a map and describe its geographical context
  • Explain the cultural and–or natural significance of the site
  • Describe the management practices that protect the site
  • Identify and explain the threats the site faces
  • Produce a clearly structured information report using geographical vocabulary

Lesson Structure

1

Revision Focus

Whole class

Students re-read their peer feedback slip from L19 and identify one specific revision they will make. Model the revision process briefly by showing students how to use peer feedback purposefully.

Revision strategy prompt

“If your partner said you could strengthen the management section, go back to your L18 research notes and look for a detail you haven’t used yet. If your partner said they wanted to know more about the threats, check whether you explained why each threat is damaging, not just listed it.”

Students identify one clear focus for their revision so they begin with a defined purpose rather than re-reading without direction. This stage takes 3–5 minutes.

Materials
  • Peer feedback slips student’s own from L19
  • Draft reports student’s own from L19
2

Revision and Completion

Individual

Extended individual writing period. Students revise and complete their reports. The revision should be purposeful: address the peer feedback, check that each section is complete, and ensure geographical vocabulary is used accurately.

Teacher conferencing priorities

  • First priority: students with incomplete drafts who need to finish. Help them identify which sections are missing and prioritise.
  • Second priority: students whose reports lack geographical vocabulary. Focus on replacing everyday language with geographical terms (using the glossary from L18 and the vocabulary bank from the L19 report structure guide).
  • Third priority: comparative students whose comparison section needs development. Help them move beyond “Site A has X but Site B doesn’t” to explain why the differences matter.

Students may reference their L17–18 research templates, the report structure guide, and the glossary during revision.

Extension

Students review their comparison section for analytical depth. Are they explaining why differences matter, or just listing them? Encourage them to add a sentence that links each comparison back to geographical significance or management challenge.

Support

Teacher conferences prioritise support students first. Focus on completion and basic structure. If a support student has a clearly structured report with geographical vocabulary, that is a strong outcome regardless of length.

Materials
  • Draft reports student’s own from L19
  • Peer feedback slips student’s own from L19
  • L17–18 Research Templates student’s own
  • Report Structure Guide from L19
  • Management and Threats Glossary from L18
  • Paper atlas final reference checks
3

Final Check and Collection

Whole class

Students complete a brief self-assessment checklist before submitting their report. The checklist is aligned to the success criteria and takes 2–3 minutes.

Reports are collected. The teacher then leads a brief whole-class reflection to link the World Heritage investigation back to the unit arc:

Reflection questions

  • “What did you learn about your site that surprised you?”
  • “What geographical skill from earlier in the unit helped you most during this investigation?”

Students are reminded that the skills they used (atlas navigation, geographical vocabulary, evidence-based writing, cause-and-effect reasoning) are the same skills they will apply in the summative task (Design a Place, L21–24). This foreshadowing is deliberate.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Are students making purposeful revisions based on peer feedback (not just re-reading and declaring it done)?
  • Do final reports have a clear structure with distinct sections?
  • Is geographical vocabulary used accurately and naturally?
  • Can students articulate what they learned during the reflection?

How to capture

Final reports are the collected assessment artefact. Teacher notes which students needed significant conferencing in this lesson, which students produced strong reports independently, and which students struggled with the self-assessment. These notes inform conferencing priorities for the summative task (L21–24).

What it tells you

The information report is formative preparation for the summative task. Students who write well-structured reports with clear geographical reasoning are ready for independent work in L21–24. Students who needed heavy conferencing support will need early and frequent check-ins during the summative planning phase.
