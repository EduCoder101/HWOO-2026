Learning Intention

Produce and explain a geographically informed map using correct conventions

Success Criteria

  • Create a detailed map using all required conventions: title, key, compass rose, scale bar, grid and labels
  • Write a geographical explanation justifying location and design decisions
  • Reflect on which geographical concepts from the unit informed the design

Lesson Structure

1

Map Creation

Individual

Students create their designed map, working from the planning document and annotated sketch completed in L21–22. Before beginning, distribute the map conventions checklist and ask students to keep it visible as they work.

The map must include all conventions taught across the unit:

  • Title: clear and descriptive
  • Key: colour-coded, showing different feature types (physical features, human features, land use zones, services, transport)
  • Compass rose: showing at least cardinal directions
  • Scale representation: this does not need to be precisely calculated – a simple scale bar or written statement of scale (e.g. “1 cm represents approximately 1 km”) is sufficient. The emphasis is on demonstrating understanding that maps represent real distances, not on mathematical accuracy.
  • Grid references: a basic grid overlay with labelled axes so that features can be located using references
  • Labels: clear labels for major features, regions or zones

Students work on their map template (A3 bordered sheet with a key panel on the right side). Encourage students to draft lightly in pencil first, then add colour and detail. The atlas remains available as a reference for how real maps represent similar features.

Extension

Students include a “Changes Over Time” annotation layer – small notes on their map indicating how the place might change in the future and why (drawing on environmental impact reasoning from L13–14).

Support

Provide a pre-gridded map template with the key panel pre-structured (category headings already in place: Physical Features, Human Features, Services, Transport). Student fills in the specific features rather than designing the key from scratch.

2

Written Geographical Explanation

Individual

Students write their geographical explanation on the writing guide provided. The explanation must include one paragraph per component, as a minimum:

  1. Physical environment: Describe the natural features of the place and explain how the physical environment influenced design decisions.
  2. Human features: Describe the human features included and explain why they are needed at this scale.
  3. Services and infrastructure: Explain what services and systems the place needs to function and how they connect.
  4. Location decisions: Justify why features are located where they are. What geographical reasoning underpins the spatial layout?

Students should use geographical vocabulary naturally throughout. By this point in the unit, terms like physical feature, human feature, land use, services, infrastructure, population, settlement, topography and scale should be part of their active vocabulary.

The writing guide provides the four section headings and a brief prompt for each, but does not include sentence starters. Students are expected to write independently at this stage.

Extension

Add a fifth paragraph: “Reflection.” Students reflect on which geographical concepts from the unit most influenced their design. This is explicitly called for in success criterion 3 and rewards students who can think metacognitively about their learning.

Support

Provide the writing guide with sentence starters for each paragraph (e.g. “The physical environment of my place includes…”). These are optional scaffolds, not required structures.

Materials
  • Writing guide printable template below, one per student
3

Peer Share and Submission

Pairs, then individual

Structured peer share. Each student pairs with a partner (teacher assigns or students choose). Partners exchange their completed map and explanation. Each partner reads the explanation, examines the map, and uses the assessment rubric (from L21–22) to provide feedback.

The feedback protocol:

  • Identify one strength: something the partner has done well, referencing a specific rubric criterion
  • Identify one suggestion: something that could be improved, again referencing the rubric
  • Write the feedback on a peer feedback slip

After receiving feedback, students have remaining time to make final revisions. This mirrors the peer feedback protocol from L19 (World Heritage drafting) – students have practised this process.

Teacher collects all work for summative assessment: the map, the written explanation, the planning document (from L21–22), and the peer feedback slip.

Key question: Looking at your partner’s work, what geographical thinking can you see in their design?

Atlas Work

▾

The atlas serves as a reference tool only in these lessons. Students consult it to verify their map conventions are accurate and to check how real maps at their chosen scale represent features. For example, a student designing a city-scale place might check the Sydney atlas map to see how the key distinguishes residential from commercial areas. Atlas use is self-directed and integrated into the work, not a separate task.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Map includes all required conventions (title, key, compass rose, scale representation, grid, labels)
  • Key is colour-coded and distinguishes different feature categories
  • Layout demonstrates spatial reasoning – features are placed for geographical reasons
  • Written explanation uses geographical vocabulary and provides reasoning for design decisions
  • Written explanation addresses all four components (physical environment, human features, services, location decisions)
  • Peer feedback references specific rubric criteria

How to capture

Mark against the rubric distributed in L21–22. Note the quality of peer feedback (it indicates depth of geographical understanding). Use the rubric’s five criteria: geographical understanding, map conventions, spatial logic, written explanation, presentation.

What it tells you

This is the summative assessment for the unit. Results inform reporting on HS3-GEO-01, HS3-GEO-02 and GE3-4. Students who achieve “Beginning” on multiple criteria may need additional support in future geography units.
