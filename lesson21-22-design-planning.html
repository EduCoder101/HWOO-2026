Success Criteria

Lesson Structure

1

Task Introduction and Rubric Review

Whole class

Introduce the Design a Place summative task. This is the culminating assessment for the unit – students independently design a place at a chosen scale, produce a map using all the conventions they have learned, and write a geographical explanation justifying their decisions.

Display the assessment rubric on the interactive whiteboard and walk through each criterion. The rubric assesses five areas: breadth of geographical understanding (whether the place demonstrates awareness of physical features, human features, services, transport and infrastructure), map conventions (title, key, compass rose, scale representation, grid references and labels), spatial logic (whether the layout of the place makes geographical sense), written explanation (use of geographical vocabulary and reasoning to justify decisions), and presentation quality.

Emphasise that this task rewards breadth of understanding rather than technical precision. For example, including a scale representation on the map is important, but the task is not asking students to draw distances to exact measurements. What matters is that students can think geographically about what makes a place function – the utilities a city needs, the services, transport connections, environmental considerations.

Reference specific examples from the unit to make each criterion concrete: “Think about what we learned about Sydney’s land use zones – your place will need different areas for different purposes” or “Remember how physical features shaped where people settled in Sydney – your physical environment should influence where you put things.”

Key question: What makes a place work? What does a place need in order to function well for the people who live there?

2

Scale Choice and Planning Document

Individual

Students choose their scale: suburb, city or country. The choice must be justified geographically – not simply “I want to do a city because it’s bigger” but reasoning that connects to geographical concepts: “I chose city scale because I want to show how different land use zones connect, and you need city scale to see that pattern.”

Once scale is chosen, students work through the five-section planning document. Each section addresses a core geographical question:

  • Physical environment: What natural features exist in this place? What climate, landforms and water sources define it? How does the physical environment shape what can be built?
  • Human features and services: What do people need? Schools, hospitals, shops, recreation? How are these distributed across the place?
  • Infrastructure and systems: What transport connects different parts? What utilities (water, power, waste) are needed? How do different areas depend on each other?
  • Map representation: What map conventions will I use? How will I show different features in my key? What scale representation makes sense?
  • Environmental responsibility: What impact do my design decisions have on the environment? How could I minimise negative impacts?

Students use the atlas throughout to examine real-world examples at their chosen scale. For suburb scale, revisit local maps from L4. For city scale, revisit Sydney maps from L6–8. For country scale, revisit Australia maps from L10–12.

Extension

Extension students add a sixth section to their planning document: “Connections beyond my place.” They consider how their designed place connects to the wider world – trade, tourism, migration, environmental systems. This draws on the global perspective from L15.

Support

Provide a version of the planning document with sentence starters and guiding prompts in each section. Narrow the scale choice to suburb (the most concrete and familiar scale) unless the student has a strong preference otherwise.

3

Teacher Conferencing and Planning Sketch

Individual

Teacher conferences with each student while the class works. The conferencing checklist covers three key checkpoints:

  1. Has the student chosen a scale and provided a geographical justification?
  2. Has the planning document identified the required features, services and infrastructure for that scale?
  3. Is the student beginning to think spatially – where things will go and why?

Students who complete their planning document move to producing an annotated planning sketch. This is a rough spatial layout showing where major features will be located. It does not need formal map conventions yet (those come in L23–24), but it should show the student’s thinking about spatial relationships: why certain features are adjacent, how transport routes connect areas, where the physical environment constrains or enables development.

Students who have not completed a satisfactory plan by the end of L22 should be flagged – they will need additional support before beginning map creation in L23.

Key question: Why have you put things where they are? What geographical reasoning is behind your layout?

Atlas Work

▾

Students use the atlas as a reference throughout the planning process. The atlas is not a separate task but an integrated research tool. Students examine real-world examples at their chosen scale: suburb-scale students look at local area maps (as in L4), city-scale students look at Sydney regional maps (L6–8), and country-scale students look at Australia maps (L10–12). The atlas helps students understand what features, services and infrastructure exist at different scales in real places, informing their design decisions.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Scale choice is justified with geographical reasoning (not just preference)
  • Planning document addresses all five sections with substantive geographical content
  • Student can articulate why features are located where they are in the sketch
  • Student uses geographical vocabulary naturally (land use, physical features, infrastructure, services)

How to capture

Conferencing checklist (one row per student). Note scale choice, planning completeness, and any support needs. Flag students who are not ready for map creation.

What it tells you

Identifies students who need additional scaffolding before L23. A student whose plan is thin or whose sketch lacks spatial reasoning will struggle with the final map and explanation. Use the conferencing data to plan targeted support for L23–24.
