  • Small-group scaffolded discussion: groups investigate how their sites are managed and what threatens them, using resource packs and the glossary

Learning Intention

Investigate a World Heritage Site and produce an information report on its location, significance, management and threats

Success Criteria

  • Locate the chosen World Heritage Site on a map and describe its geographical context
  • Explain the cultural and/or natural significance of the site
  • Describe the management practices that protect the site
  • Identify and explain the threats the site faces
  • Produce a clearly structured information report using geographical vocabulary

Lesson Structure

1

Worked Example and Glossary

Whole class

Today’s focus: how is your site protected, and what threatens it? Before students begin their own research, the class works through a management and threats analysis together using a site that is not on the investigation list.

Worked example: Stonehenge

Display the Stonehenge worked example on the board or distribute copies. Walk through the analysis together:

  • Management: Who manages it? (English Heritage, with UNESCO oversight.) What systems are in place? (Visitor management – timed entry, walkways to prevent foot traffic on the stones. Funding – ticket sales and government grants. Conservation – ongoing archaeological research and stone preservation. Laws – it is a Scheduled Ancient Monument, protected by UK law.) What is the management trying to achieve? (Balance public access with preservation.)
  • Threats: What threatens it? (Road traffic – a major road used to run very close to the site. Tourism pressure – millions of visitors each year. Environmental erosion – weathering of the stones over time. Development – pressure from nearby housing and infrastructure projects.)

Key insight

Management exists because of threats. The same activity that threatens a site (tourism, development) is often the reason people care about it and fund its management.

Introduce the management and threats glossary. Students keep the glossary for reference during their own research.

Materials
  • Stonehenge Worked Example printable template below, projected or one per student
  • Management and Threats Glossary printable template below, one per student
2

Group Research: Management and Threats

Small groups (3–4), then individual

Students form small groups (3–4) based on similar sites or site types (e.g. natural sites together, cultural sites together). This grouping is deliberate: management and threats concepts are complex, and discussing them with peers helps students process source material that may be above their independent reading level.

Groups work through their curated resource packs and library books, focusing on management and threats. The group discussion is scaffolded by the glossary: when students encounter an unfamiliar concept, they check the glossary first. The teacher circulates and facilitates group discussions, asking probing questions: “Who pays for the protection of this site?” “What would happen if the management stopped?”

After the group discussion phase (approximately 15–20 minutes), students transition to individual recording. Each student completes the management and threats sections of their own L18 research template. The group discussion has prepared them to write, but the recording is individual.

Extension

Students investigating two sites note comparison points in their research template. They consider whether the same management approach could work for both sites, and why or why not.

Support

Groups including support students are given the research template with sentence starters. The glossary is their primary support tool. Teacher prioritises these groups for conferencing.

Materials
  • L18 Research Template printable template below, one per student
  • Curated resource packs or library books continues from L16–17
  • Management and Threats Glossary continues from Stage 1
  • Paper atlas for reference as needed
3

Consolidation Discussion

Whole class

Brief whole-class discussion. Two or three students share one threat their site faces and one management response. The teacher draws out the key conceptual point: management exists because of threats. Protection and damage are connected – the same activity that threatens a site (tourism, development, resource use) is often the reason people care about it and fund its management.

Key question

If no one visited your site, would it still need protection? Why?

This connects to the cause-and-consequence reasoning students practised in L13 (Murray-Darling Basin) and L14 (Borneo Palm Oil). The analytical skill is the same; the context is different.

Remind students that by the end of L18, they should have complete research notes covering all four report sections: context (L17), significance (L17), management (L18), threats (L18). These notes are the raw material for drafting in L19.

Materials
  • No additional materials

Reference

Teaching Notes

▾

Why the worked example matters

Management concepts are abstract for Year 5. The worked example exists to make these concrete before students encounter them in their own research. Do not skip the worked example. Students see the thinking process modelled with Stonehenge before they apply it to their own sites.

Why small groups work here

Sources about World Heritage management are rarely written for primary-aged readers. Group discussion helps students process complex text collaboratively. Monitor groups to ensure all students are contributing, not just one strong reader. Ask follow-up questions to each group member, not just the group as a whole.

The glossary as a working tool

The glossary bridges the vocabulary gap. Encourage students to use it actively during research, not just read it once. When conferencing, ask students to point to glossary terms they have used in their notes. A student who can define “conservation” or “development pressure” in their own context is deepening their understanding.

Connection to prior learning

The connection to L13–14 (environmental impact reasoning) is important. Students have already practised cause-and-consequence thinking about human impact on the Murray-Darling Basin and Borneo. L18 extends this to a new context. Reference these prior lessons explicitly when modelling the Stonehenge example.

Atlas Work

▾

Atlas use in L18 is reference-based rather than investigative. Students may check the atlas to clarify physical geography relevant to threats (e.g. a coastal site and sea level rise, a site near agricultural land and development pressure). Atlas navigation is incidental rather than the focus. The primary work in L18 is reading and discussing sources about management and threats.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Can students name specific management practices (not just “it is protected”)?
  • Are students explaining why something is a threat, using cause-and-effect reasoning?
  • Are small-group discussions productive, with all students contributing?
  • Are extension students noting meaningful comparison points?

How to capture

Research templates are the primary artefact. Teacher notes which groups needed significant facilitation and which students could not transition effectively from group discussion to individual recording. These notes inform conferencing priorities in L19 (students who struggled to record independently will need support transitioning to report drafting).

What it tells you

Students who produce detailed management and threats notes with cause-and-effect reasoning are ready to draft. Students whose notes list threats without explanation will need conferencing at the start of L19 to deepen their reasoning before writing. The group discussion quality is diagnostic: groups that could not discuss management independently suggest the conceptual scaffolding needs more time in L19.

Worked Example – Stonehenge (United Kingdom)

Cultural Heritage, listed 1986

Management
Governance: Managed by English Heritage, with UNESCO oversight
Conservation: Ongoing archaeological research and stone preservation programme
Tourism management: Timed entry system, walking paths to protect the stones, visitor centre set back from the monument
Funding: Ticket sales, government grants, and charitable donations
Threats
Road traffic: A major road (A303) runs very close to the site, causing noise and visual intrusion. A tunnel project is underway.
Tourism pressure: Over 1.5 million visitors each year. Foot traffic and vibrations affect the stones.
Environmental erosion: Natural weathering of the stones over centuries
Development pressure: Nearby housing and infrastructure proposals
The Connection

Management exists because of threats. Without the road, the traffic management would not be needed. Without the tourists, the timed entry system would not exist.

World Heritage Investigation – Key Terms

Governance
Who is in charge of managing and making decisions about the site
Conservation
The work done to protect and look after the site so it survives for the future
Funding
Where the money comes from to pay for management and conservation
Tourism management
Controlling how many people visit and what they can do at the site
Legislation
Laws that protect the site from damage or development
Erosion
The gradual wearing away of rock, soil, or structures by wind, water, or human activity
Invasive species
Plants or animals that do not naturally belong in an area and cause damage to local ecosystems
Climate change
Long-term changes in weather patterns that affect the environment (e.g. rising sea levels, coral bleaching, more extreme weather)
Development pressure
The expansion of buildings, roads, farms, or industry near a protected site
Resource extraction
Taking natural resources (minerals, water, timber) from an area, often causing environmental damage
