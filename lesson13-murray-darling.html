Lesson 13 · Human Impact: Murray-Darling Basin

How farming and water management decisions have reshaped Australia’s largest river system
Lesson at a Glance
  • Students locate the Murray-Darling Basin in the atlas and examine satellite imagery showing the effects of irrigation and land degradation
  • Pairs investigate using four teacher-curated fact cards covering agriculture, water extraction, environmental consequences, and Indigenous connection
  • Class discussion explores responsibility: who made these decisions, who has been affected, and who should act
  • Individual written response: 3–5 sentences explaining how farming decisions have changed the Basin

Learning Intention

Explain how farming and water management decisions have changed the Murray-Darling Basin

Success Criteria

  • Locate the Murray-Darling Basin on a map and describe its physical geography
  • Explain how irrigation and farming have changed the Basin’s environment
  • Describe at least two consequences of these changes for communities and ecosystems
  • Begin to articulate who bears responsibility when a shared natural resource is damaged

Lesson Structure

1

The Basin

Whole class

Students locate the Murray-Darling Basin in their atlas using the Australia physical map and river systems map. They trace the river network from source to mouth, noting the Basin spans four states and covers over one million square kilometres.

Google Maps satellite view is projected on the IWB. The teacher zooms into three contrasting areas: a healthy river section with intact vegetation, an area of irrigated farmland (cotton or rice), and a visibly degraded area showing dry riverbeds or salt-affected soil. Students observe and describe what they see before any explanation is given.

Brief orientation: the Murray-Darling produces about one-third of Australia’s food supply. This fact establishes the stakes before the investigation begins.

Key question: What has happened to this river system, and why?

Materials
  • Paper atlas Australia physical & river systems maps
  • Google Maps satellite view IWB projection, pre-loaded
2

Fact Card Investigation

Pairs

Pairs investigate using teacher-curated fact cards. Each set contains four A5 laminated cards covering distinct topics.

Card topics:

  • “Australia’s Food Bowl” – The Basin’s role in Australian agriculture, scale of irrigation, production figures.
  • “Turning on the Taps” – History of water extraction from early 1900s, dams and weirs, Snowy Mountains Scheme, rise beyond sustainable levels.
  • “What Went Wrong” – Environmental consequences: reduced flows, rising salinity, wetland loss, algal blooms, fish kills, before-and-after evidence.
  • “River as Country” – Indigenous peoples’ deep connection, impact of water extraction on communities, cultural water allocations advocacy.

Each pair reads all four cards but records detailed findings on two cards (assigned by teacher). This creates genuine information-sharing in the discussion that follows. Students record findings on a structured recording sheet with headings matching the four card topics.

Extension

Students add a paragraph addressing the responsibility question – who should be responsible for restoring the Basin, and why?

Support

The recording sheet includes sentence starters under each heading. Students may focus on two cards rather than skimming all four.

Materials
  • Fact card sets 4 × A5 per pair, laminated; printable template below
  • Recording sheet A4, one per student; printable template below
3

Discussion and Written Response

Whole class then individual

Each pair shares one key finding from their investigation. This ensures all fact card content is surfaced before the discussion questions open.

Class discussion structured around three questions:

  • Who made these decisions?
  • Who has been affected?
  • Who is responsible for fixing it?

Individual written response: students write 3–5 sentences explaining how farming decisions have changed the Murray-Darling Basin and who has been affected. This is collected as the lesson assessment.

Sentence starters available as support: “The Murray-Darling Basin has been changed by…” / “One consequence of this is…” / “The people most affected include…”

Support

Sentence starters sheet (A4 2-up) provided to all students who need it. Printable template below.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Can students trace the Basin’s extent on the atlas and describe its physical geography?
  • Do pairs engage substantively with all four fact cards, or skim the surface?
  • Does discussion show cause-and-effect reasoning, not just description?
  • Does the written response include at least two specific consequences?

How to capture

Phase 3 observation checklist. Note students who struggle with cause-and-effect language – they will need additional support for the L14 cross-case comparison.

What it tells you

Students who can identify consequences but cannot explain the chain of cause and effect will need a more structured scaffold for L14’s comparison task. The written response is the primary data source.

Fact Card Sets

Australia’s Food Bowl
  • The Murray-Darling Basin covers more than one million square kilometres across four states
  • About one-third of Australia’s food is produced in the Basin
  • Major crops include cotton, rice, wheat, and citrus fruits
  • Over 2 million Australians depend on the Basin for their water supply
  • The Basin contains over 30,000 wetlands that support native plants and animals
[Teacher: insert image]
What would happen to Australia’s food supply if the rivers stopped flowing?
Turning on the Taps
  • From the early 1900s, governments built dams, weirs, and irrigation channels to redirect water for farming
  • The Snowy Mountains Scheme (1949–1974) diverted rivers inland to increase irrigation
  • By the 1990s, more water was being taken out of the rivers than nature could replace
  • During severe droughts, some sections of the river system stopped flowing entirely
  • The Murray-Darling Basin Plan (2012) tried to set limits on water extraction, but remains controversial
[Teacher: insert image]
If everyone agreed the rivers needed more water, why was it so hard to change?
What Went Wrong
  • Reduced river flows mean less water for wetlands, floodplains, and native species
  • Rising salinity (salt levels) has damaged soil and made some farmland unusable
  • Algal blooms caused by low water levels and nutrient runoff have poisoned waterways
  • In 2019, up to one million fish died near Menindee in a mass fish kill linked to low oxygen levels
  • The Coorong wetland at the Murray mouth has shrunk dramatically, threatening migratory bird species
[Teacher: insert image]
When we see the consequences, should we stop farming in the Basin entirely?
River as Country
  • For Aboriginal peoples, the Murray-Darling river system is Country – a living system with deep cultural, spiritual, and practical significance
  • The rivers and wetlands have sustained Aboriginal communities for tens of thousands of years, providing food, fresh water, and places for ceremony
  • Water extraction has damaged culturally significant wetlands, reduced fish stocks that communities depend on, and disrupted traditional land management practices
  • Aboriginal leaders have advocated for “cultural water allocations” – water set aside specifically to maintain the health of Country
  • The Barkandji, Ngarrindjeri, Yorta Yorta, and many other nations continue to fight for the river’s health
[Teacher: insert image]
Whose voice should matter most when decisions are made about a shared river system?
