Map Skills: Cardinal Directions and Symbols
Lesson at a Glance
  • Teach compass rose: students draw their own compass rose in books following a video (cardinal then intercardinal), then handle real compasses to identify north and orient to called directions
  • Compass direction puzzle: students identify five objects from their desk with compass direction and distance, then swap desks and solve their partner's directions
  • Quick consolidation of map symbols and keys using atlas and local street map
  • HTML symbol challenge: pairs on one device, self-checking symbol identification quiz – teacher circulates and observes pair reasoning
  • Stride calibration on the balcony: students walk a marked 10m distance, count strides, calculate personal stride length
  • Outdoor orienteering race on the courts: pairs follow printed instruction cards using compass directions and stride-calculated distances across four routes, revealing the phrase MAPS SHOW OUR WORLD

Learning Intention

Use cardinal and intercardinal directions and read map symbols and keys.

Success Criteria

  • Use N, S, E, W, NE, SE, SW and NW correctly on a map
  • Read and explain at least five symbols from a map key
  • Give directions between two locations using compass points
  • Navigate a real-world route using compass directions and a printed instruction card

Lesson Structure

1

Compass and Symbols

Classroom · Whole class then individual/pairs

Compass rose labelling

Students draw their own compass rose following the compass rose drawing video (project on the interactive whiteboard). Work through cardinal points first (N, S, E, W), then intercardinal (NE, SE, SW, NW). Students draw and label in their books as the video progresses. This produces a better result than a printed template and gives students ownership of the tool they'll use throughout the unit.

Compass handling

Teacher demonstrates how to hold and read a compass correctly. Pairs then use a real compass to identify north in the room and orient to teacher-called directions. Confirm the whole class can reliably orient before moving on.

Compass direction puzzle – create and swap

Each student identifies five objects visible from their desk and records, for each: the object name, its compass direction, and an approximate distance in metres (e.g. teacher's desk – NE, approx. 5m). Students then swap desks and attempt to solve their partner's directions using a compass. Partners debrief: were the directions accurate enough to find each object? This is the first time students generate directions rather than receive them – expect some imprecision, and use it as a teaching moment.

Extension
During the compass puzzle: students must also estimate and record the distance to each object in metres before checking with a ruler or stride – adds measurement precision to direction accuracy.
Support
Provide a pre-labelled compass rose (cardinal points N, S, E, W already filled in) for students to complete by adding intercardinal points only. During the compass puzzle, allow supported students to work with a partner rather than independently.

Map symbols and key

Quick consolidation – students have encountered the atlas key in Lesson 1. Using the local street map and atlas, identify a set of symbols together. Establish the principle: every map has a key; a symbol means nothing without it. Move at pace – prior knowledge means this does not need extended treatment.

2

HTML Symbol Challenge

Classroom · Pairs on one device

Pairs on one device. Students work through the HTML symbol challenge – each correct identification unlocks the next clue. Wrong answers require a retry; there is no penalty and no teacher intervention needed for resets. The self-checking mechanism allows the teacher to circulate and observe pair reasoning rather than manage answers.

Transition: before moving outside, pairs check compasses are working and collect their orienteering card for their starting route. Teacher confirms the outdoor space is ready.

3

Orienteering Race

Outdoor · Pairs

Stride calibration – balcony (whole class)

Before splitting onto routes, the whole class moves to the balcony. Each student walks the marked 10m distance at a natural pace, counting strides. Students record their stride count on the phrase slip and calculate their personal stride length (10 ÷ number of strides). Pairs discuss: if a card says '15 metres north-east,' how many of your strides is that? Confirm everyone has a usable stride length before moving to the courts.

Orienteering race – courts

Brief the task clearly before releasing pairs: follow printed instruction cards using compass directions and stride-calculated distances; each correct arrival reveals the next card; Card 6 reveals a word. Fast-finishing pairs collect a new route card and continue toward MAPS SHOW OUR WORLD. The school grounds map is available from the teacher if a pair is genuinely stuck – not issued automatically.

Pairs start on different routes (stagger if needed to avoid crowding at early stations). Teacher circulates – observe compass use and stride counting, not just arrival at locations.

Extension
Fast pairs complete all four routes and assemble MAPS SHOW OUR WORLD, then write one sentence explaining what the phrase means geographically.
Support
Pair with a confident navigator and assign shorter routes first (A and B). School grounds map available without request.

Close: Pairs record revealed word(s) on the phrase slip. Return to class. Fast-finishing pairs share the phrase MAPS SHOW OUR WORLD – brief class discussion on what it means as a geography principle.

Reference

Teaching Notes

▾

Compass misconception to address explicitly

Students frequently confuse 'face north' with 'the top of the map is where I'm standing.' Establish early: the compass tells you where north is in the real world; the map key tells you how north is represented on paper. Address this directly during the compass-handling segment – do not leave it to emerge later.

Compass puzzle – expect imprecision

When students first generate directions rather than follow them, approximations will be rough. The debrief between partners – 'I couldn't find the window because your direction was wrong' – is where much of the learning happens. Create space for this conversation rather than moving on immediately.

Outdoor setup – non-negotiable pre-lesson task

Two things must be done before students arrive outdoors: mark the 10m balcony distance with chalk or tape (must be accurate), and place all 24 orienteering cards at their correct locations. If cards are out of position the task breaks immediately. Confirm card placement is not visible from start points. Allow 15–20 minutes for setup.

Materials & Resources

▾

Stage 1 – Classroom

  • Compass rose drawing video – youtube.com/watch?v=swXwvbN_TC8. Project on interactive whiteboard. Students draw along in their books. Test playback before the lesson.
  • Pre-labelled compass rose (support) – A5, cardinal points already filled in. Teacher-created.
  • Real compasses – one per pair, minimum. Sourced from school resources.
  • Atlas – one per student. NSW/Sydney map for symbol and key work.
  • Printed local street map – A4 or A3, Roseville/North Shore, minimum ten identifiable landmarks, compass rose and map key with at least eight symbols. Confirm landmarks are visible at print scale before the lesson.
  • Interactive whiteboard – for displaying compass rose during explicit teaching.

Stage 2 – HTML Symbol Challenge

  • Device – one per pair, tablet or laptop. Requires reliable WiFi. Load and test the HTML file on school devices before the lesson.
  • HTML Symbol Challenge file – teacher-created interactive file. Symbol identification questions drawn from the atlas key; correct answer unlocks next clue; wrong answer prompts retry. To be built and tested separately from this lesson plan.

Stage 3 – Outdoor Orienteering

  • Orienteering instruction cards – 24 cards total. Four routes (A, B, C, D) × six cards each. Each card states: route letter, card number, compass direction, distance in metres. Card 6 on each route carries one word: Route A → MAPS, Route B → SHOW, Route C → OUR, Route D → WORLD. Laminated or printed on card stock. Placed at correct locations before the lesson – allow 15–20 minutes for setup.
  • Printed school grounds map – simple A4 overhead-view of the hall or courts with key landmarks. Scaffold only – available from the teacher on request, not issued by default.
  • Real compasses – same set as Stage 1.
  • Phrase recording slip – one per pair. Two sections: (1) stride calibration fields – stride count over 10m and calculated stride length; (2) phrase assembly grid – one field per route word, plus full phrase. Teacher-created, fits on A5.
  • 10m marked distance on balcony – chalk or tape, measured accurately. Set up during card placement preparation.

Atlas Work

▾

When: Stage 1 – map symbols and key segment.

Task: Students locate the NSW/Sydney map in the atlas. Using the map key, identify a minimum of eight symbols – students annotate a small printed list confirming the symbol name and what it represents. This is the same atlas map used in Lesson 1; students should be able to locate it independently.

Page reference: Confirm against school atlas edition before the lesson and insert here.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Stage 1 – compass handling: Can students orient to a called direction using a real compass, or are they guessing?
  • Stage 1 – compass puzzle: Are the directions students write precise enough for a partner to solve? Vague directions signal the concept has not yet landed.
  • Stage 2: Are pairs reasoning about symbols or just clicking through? Listen to pair conversation.
  • Stage 3: Are students consulting the compass before moving, or moving and then checking? The former indicates genuine navigation.

How to capture

Phase 1 Observation Checklist. Columns: Uses cardinal & intercardinal directions · Reads map symbols & key. Code during Stage 1 compass handling and Stage 3. Use Stage 2 (self-checking) as circulation and coding time.

What it tells you

Any student who cannot reliably orient a compass by the end of Stage 1 will struggle with grid references in Lesson 3. Flag these students – a brief one-to-one compass check at the start of L3 may be needed.
