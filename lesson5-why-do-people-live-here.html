Lesson 5 · Why Do People Live Here?

Understanding the pull factors that attract people to a place
Lesson at a Glance
  • Pull factors formally introduced using the North Shore as a familiar anchor, then broadened: why do people choose to live WHERE they live?
  • Five ‘Voices from Different Places’ quote cards – students decode pull factors from first-person accounts across coastal, urban, regional, rural, and inland settings
  • Group investigation: identify and categorise pull factors from each voice, then compare patterns across places
  • Whole-class synthesis: what matters to different people? Could you be happy in Dubbo? Could the farmer be happy here?
  • Individual geographical explanation about the North Shore using two sentence stems: ‘People choose to live here because…’ and ‘This matters because…’

Learning Intention

Explain why people choose to live in a particular place

Success Criteria

  • Identify at least three factors that attract people to a place (services, environment, connections)
  • Give reasons why people live on Sydney’s North Shore
  • Write a geographical explanation using the sentence starter: ‘People choose to live here because…’

Lesson Structure

1

Pull Factors

8 min · Whole class

Open with the question: Why do people choose to live where they live? Not just here – anywhere. What makes someone pick one place over another? Take a few quick responses. Students will likely default to their own experience at first. That is expected.

Introduce the term pull factors: the features of a place that attract people to live there. Explain that geographers group pull factors into categories to understand patterns, not just list individual things. Introduce four categories:

  • Services – schools, hospitals, shops, libraries
  • Environment – parks, beaches, bushland, clean air, open space
  • Transport and connections – train stations, bus routes, roads, proximity to work or the city
  • Community – family, sporting clubs, places of worship, neighbours, cultural groups

Use a brief Google Maps recap to ground the categories in the familiar. Display the North Shore with the amenities/points-of-interest layer visible. Zoom into two or three areas and ask: What pull factors can you see here? Which category would they fall into? Keep this short – students already know the area from L4. The purpose is to connect the new vocabulary to familiar geography before broadening the lens.

Frame what comes next: The North Shore is the place we know. But not everyone lives here, and not everyone would want to. We are going to hear from five young people who live in very different parts of Australia, and your job is to work out what pull factors attracted their families to those places.

Materials
  • Google Maps North Shore with amenities layer, pre-loaded on IWB/projector
2

Voices from Different Places

20 min · Small groups (3–4)

Distribute the set of five quote cards and the investigation recording sheet (one set per group). Each card is a short first-person account from a Year 5 student explaining why their family lives where they live. The five voices represent different place types: Sydney coastal, western Sydney suburban, inland regional town, coastal regional, and rural farming. Pull factors are embedded in the language – not labelled. Students must decode the reasoning themselves.

The Task

Step 1 – Decode. For each voice, groups identify: Where does this person live? What type of place is it? What pull factors can you find in what they said? They record the pull factors and categorise them using the four categories from Stage 1. Some pull factors will not fit neatly into one category (e.g. affordability, family ties, work opportunities). That is deliberate – encourage groups to discuss where these belong or whether they need a new category.

Step 2 – Compare. Groups look across all five voices and note patterns: Which pull factors appear in more than one place? Which are unique to one place type? What matters to one person that doesn’t seem to matter to another? The recording sheet has space for groups to note 2–3 observations about patterns and differences.

Circulate and push thinking: You’ve written ‘beach’ for Zara. Is beach the pull factor, or is it something deeper? What does the beach give her family? Also ask: Did anyone notice something in Karim’s card about why his family chose Merrylands? What about Anh’s family – what made them move to Coffs Harbour?

The atlas should be available for reference. Groups can look up where each place is and use compass directions and grid references to describe the locations. This reinforces L2–3 skills in an investigation context.

Extension

Groups add a sixth voice: they write 2–3 sentences from the perspective of someone who lives on the North Shore, in the same style as the quote cards. This requires them to identify their own area’s pull factors and articulate them as a first-person account. Ask: Would your card sound different from someone else’s on the North Shore? Why?

Support

Provide a word bank listing pull factor terms (services, environment, transport, community, affordability, family, work, lifestyle, space). Narrow the task: complete Step 1 for three cards before attempting Step 2. Pre-fill one row on the recording sheet to model how to decode a quote into pull factors.

3

Why Here? Why There?

7 min · Whole class

Bring the class together. Ask: What did you notice? Were the pull factors the same everywhere, or different? Take 3–4 responses that highlight the diversity of reasons.

Push deeper: Could the farmer near Moree be happy living on the North Shore? Could you be happy living in Dubbo? Why or why not? The purpose is to surface the meta-outcome: pull factors are personal and contextual. What attracts one family to a place depends on what they need – work, family, space, services, affordability, lifestyle. No place is universally “best”; different people value different things.

Ask: Did anyone notice that some families moved to their place, while others have been there for generations? What does that tell us about pull factors – do they only matter when you first choose a place, or do they keep you there too?

Connect forward: When we look at Sydney as a whole next lesson, we will see that different parts of the city attract different people for different reasons. The thinking you have done today – decoding pull factors and comparing places – is exactly what geographers do when they study why communities form where they do.

Materials
  • No additional materials
4

Geographical Explanation

15 min · Individual

Students write individually. The task: write a geographical explanation of 3–5 sentences answering the question Why do people choose to live on the North Shore?

Provide two sentence stems on the board or on the writing section of the recording sheet:

  • People choose to live on the North Shore because…
  • This matters because…

The first stem asks students to identify a pull factor. The second pushes them to explain why that factor attracts people – the reasoning beneath the observation. Model this briefly: “People choose to live on the North Shore because there are beaches and national parks nearby. This matters because families want outdoor spaces where children can play and people can exercise without travelling far.” Point out that the second sentence is where the geographical thinking happens.

Students should aim for at least two pull factor/explanation pairs, drawing on what they know from L4 and what they have learned from comparing places today. Stronger responses will reference a contrasting place to strengthen the explanation (e.g. “Unlike Dubbo, the North Shore is close to the city, which matters because many families need to commute to work”).

Collect all writing. This is the first collected written assessment in the unit and sets a baseline for geographical explanation quality. The AI feedback prompt (provided in the programme document) can be used to generate written feedback for each student’s reflection.

Extension

Students add a final sentence: However, some people might not choose to live here because… This brings in the trade-off thinking from Stage 3 and produces a more balanced geographical explanation. Encourage students to draw on the quote cards for evidence.

Support

Provide the sentence frame as a printed slip: “People choose to live on the North Shore because _______. This matters because _______.” Students complete two copies of this frame, one for each pull factor. This structures the writing without reducing the thinking demand – they still need to choose a pull factor and explain its significance.

Atlas Work

▾

Groups use the Sydney/NSW atlas page in Stage 2 to locate the five places mentioned in the quote cards and describe their positions using compass directions and grid references from L2–3. The atlas grounds the quotes in real geography – students can see how far Dubbo is from Sydney, where Coffs Harbour sits on the coast, and how the North Shore relates to western Sydney. Encourage groups to note the place type (coastal, inland, rural) alongside the location.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Can students decode pull factors from the quote cards, or do they just restate what the person said without identifying the underlying reason?
  • Do students categorise pull factors, or do they list them without connecting to the four categories?
  • In the comparison step, do groups notice patterns across places (e.g. “family matters everywhere” or “environment means different things in different places”)?
  • In the written explanation, do students use the second sentence stem (“This matters because…”) to provide genuine reasoning, or do they repeat the first sentence in different words?
  • Do any students reference a contrasting place to strengthen their North Shore explanation?

How to capture

Circulate during Stage 2 and note which groups decode pull factors with genuine reasoning versus surface restatement. Use a brief checklist with group names. Listen for groups that notice migration, affordability, or family ties as pull factors – these are the subtler threads in the cards. Collect all written explanations from Stage 4 – read for: (1) correct use of pull factor categories, (2) quality of reasoning in the “This matters because…” sentences, (3) whether students draw on the comparative investigation. Keep recording sheets as evidence of group thinking.

What it tells you

This assessment reveals whether students can move from description to explanation – the shift from “what’s here” to “why it matters to different people”. If students struggle with the second sentence stem, this flags a need for more scaffolded explanation writing in L6–8 before the mid-unit checkpoint at L9. If students only reference the North Shore without comparison, the comparative thinking needs reinforcing in Phase 2. Strong responses that reference contrasting places indicate readiness for the more independent geographical explanation expected in L12 and L14.

Voices from Different Places

AZara
Year 5 · Manly, Sydney

“We live near Manly, close to the beach. Mum catches the ferry to work in the city every morning – she says it’s the best commute in Sydney. There’s a surf club, a library, and my school is walking distance. On Saturdays we go to the markets. It’s busy in summer with tourists, but the rest of the year it feels like our own little town.”

BKarim
Year 5 · Merrylands, Sydney

“My family came to Australia from Lebanon before I was born. My grandparents and my aunties and uncles all live around here too, so we see them every week. There’s a really big shopping centre near us, and Dad’s warehouse is only ten minutes away, which means he’s home for dinner every night. Mum says the houses here were affordable when they first arrived.”

CLily
Year 5 · Dubbo, NSW

“We live on a property outside Dubbo. There’s so much space – I can see for ages in every direction. Dad manages cattle and Mum works at the hospital in town. It takes twenty minutes to drive to school, but I don’t mind because the roads are empty. We don’t have a beach, but we’ve got the river and the zoo, and everyone knows everyone.”

DAnh
Year 5 · Coffs Harbour, NSW

“My parents are marine biologists so we moved to Coffs Harbour for their work. It’s way smaller than Sydney but the beach is a five-minute walk. We go surfing before school sometimes. The town doesn’t have everything – Mum orders a lot of things online – but she says the lifestyle is worth it. My grandma in Vietnam says she can’t believe we live so close to the ocean.”

EJack
Year 5 · near Moree, NSW

“Our farm has been in my family for three generations. We grow wheat and canola. The nearest town is Moree, about half an hour away. I do School of the Air some days and go into town for school on others. It’s really hot in summer. My best friend lives on the next property, which is eight kilometres away. People in the city think it’s lonely but I don’t think it is.”
