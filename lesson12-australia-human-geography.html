Investigating population distribution and comparing urban and rural/remote communities

Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Explain Australia's population distribution and the services that support communities

  • Describe Australia's population distribution using geographical language
  • Explain why most Australians live near the coast
  • Compare the services available in an urban and a rural or remote community

Lesson Structure

1

Population Patterns

Whole class

Revisit the population distribution map from L11. Students open their atlases to the Australia population map and recall what they observed at Station 4 during the previous lesson.

Sharpen the observation with specific data: most Australians live within 50km of the coast. Five capital cities (Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Perth, Adelaide) account for the majority of Australia's population. Meanwhile, the vast interior – most of Australia's land area – is home to very few people.

Key question: What does daily life look like for the people who don't live in those cities? This is not a rhetorical question – students will be investigating it directly in Stage 2.

Introduce the concept that access to services varies dramatically depending on where you live. In Sydney, a hospital, school, supermarket, and fire station are typically within a short drive. In a remote community, the nearest hospital might be hundreds of kilometres away. How do communities manage when essential services are distant?

This introduction connects forward to the investigation: students are about to find out what daily life actually looks like in a rural or remote community, and compare it with what they know about Sydney.

Materials
  • Paper atlas Australia population map, one per student
2

Community Investigation

Individual

Each student selects a rural or remote Australian community from the teacher-provided curated list. The list includes twelve communities chosen for geographic spread, diversity of context, and Google Maps coverage.

Using Google Maps (satellite view and street view where available), students investigate their chosen community. They record findings in their books, guided by prompts displayed on the board:

Investigation Prompts (Display on Board)

  1. Location: Where is this community? Describe its position in Australia (state, region, distance from nearest city). Find it on your atlas population map.
  2. Services: What services can you find? Look for schools, hospitals or health centres, shops, emergency services, churches, community centres, sporting facilities.
  3. What's missing? What services would you expect in Sydney that you cannot find here? Think about variety (how many shops? what kinds?), distance (how far to the nearest hospital?), and availability (is there a high school?).
  4. Connections: What systems connect this community to the rest of Australia? Look for roads, airstrips, rail lines. Think about freight and supply routes, digital connectivity, and services like the Royal Flying Doctor Service or School of the Air.
  5. Your observation: What is one thing about this community that surprised you or that you want to share with the class?

Students spend the majority of the lesson on this investigation. Circulate to ensure students are looking beyond the satellite view – street view (where available) gives a visceral sense of what these communities actually look like.

Extension

Students add a section to their investigation addressing the question: 'Is access to services equitable across Australia? Should every community have the same services, regardless of how remote it is? What are the challenges of providing services to remote areas?'

Support

Narrow the investigation to three prompts: Location, Services, and What's Missing. Provide a simpler community selection (communities with good street view coverage and clearly visible services). Display sentence starters on the board for recording findings.

Materials
  • Curated Community List one per student or displayed, printable template below
  • Google Maps satellite and street view, one per student device
3

Discussion and Writing

Whole class then individual

Class Discussion

Brief whole-class discussion before writing. Ask 3–4 students to share what they found, focusing on patterns across different communities:

  • What services were consistently present, even in remote areas?
  • What was consistently missing or different?
  • What systems keep these communities connected to the rest of Australia?
  • What surprised you most?

The discussion surfaces common findings and helps students who may have had difficulty with their investigation. It also primes the comparison by establishing what 'urban' and 'rural/remote' mean in concrete terms, not abstractions.

Written Comparison

Students write a structured comparison of their chosen community with Sydney. Display the comparison prompts on the board:

  1. Location and population: Where is each place? How many people live there compared to Sydney?
  2. Services available: What services exist in your community? How does this compare to Sydney?
  3. Services missing or different: What can't you find in your community that you'd easily find in Sydney?
  4. Connections: How is this community connected to the rest of Australia? How does this compare to Sydney's connections?

Students write in their books. This is a collected assessment piece. The comparison should be a substantive paragraph (or short series of paragraphs) addressing all four prompts, not dot-point answers.

Remind students to use geographical vocabulary: population distribution, settlement, services, infrastructure, urban, rural, remote, coastal, interior.

Support

Provide sentence stems on the board:

  • '[Community name] is located in … , which is … kilometres from …'
  • 'Services available in [community] include … , but unlike Sydney, there is no …'
  • 'This community is connected to the rest of Australia by …'
  • 'The biggest difference between [community] and Sydney is …'
Materials
  • Board/IWB for comparison writing prompts

Atlas Work

Students revisit the Australia population distribution map from L11 at the start of the lesson. The atlas serves two purposes: (1) anchoring the population distribution concept with the visual pattern (coastal concentration, sparse interior), and (2) helping students locate their chosen community in a national context during the investigation. Students should find their community on the atlas map and note its position relative to major cities and the coast. This contextualises the Google Maps investigation by placing the community within the broader pattern they identified in L11.

Observational Assessment

What to Look For

  • Can students describe population distribution using geographical language (e.g. 'most Australians live in coastal cities' rather than 'lots of people live near the beach')?
  • During the investigation, are students looking beyond the obvious? (Not just 'there's no Woolworths' but 'the nearest hospital is 300km away and they rely on the Royal Flying Doctor Service'.)
  • Does the written comparison address all four prompts with specific evidence from the investigation?
  • Does the writing use geographical vocabulary accurately?

How to Capture

Circulate during Stage 2 to monitor investigation depth. Note students who are engaging superficially (e.g. only looking at satellite view, not exploring street view or thinking about connections). Collect the written comparison at the end of the lesson – this is the assessment artefact. Mark against the success criteria: population distribution description, coastal explanation, and urban/rural service comparison.

What It Tells You

The written comparison is the second major collected writing piece after L5. Compare quality: are students using more geographical vocabulary? Is their reasoning more evidence-based? Students who struggle with the comparison may need additional scaffolding before the L14 cross-case comparison (Murray-Darling vs Borneo), which requires comparing two unfamiliar contexts rather than comparing with familiar Sydney.
