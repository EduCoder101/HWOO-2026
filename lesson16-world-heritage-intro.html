Lesson 16 · World Heritage: Introduction and Site Selection

Understanding what makes a place globally significant, and choosing a site to investigate
Lesson at a Glance
  • Introduction to the World Heritage concept: what it means for a place to have “outstanding universal value,” who decides, and why it matters
  • Students examine a curated list of 10 World Heritage Sites from across the globe and select their site for investigation
  • Initial atlas and Google Maps orientation: students locate their chosen site, identify its geographical context, and record first observations
  • Differentiation established: support students investigate one site; core and extension students select two for comparative analysis across L17–20

Learning Intention

Investigate a World Heritage Site and produce an information report on its location, significance, management and threats

Success Criteria

  • Locate the chosen World Heritage Site on a map and describe its geographical context
  • Explain the cultural and/or natural significance of the site
  • Describe the management practices that protect the site
  • Identify and explain the threats the site faces
  • Produce a clearly structured information report using geographical vocabulary

Lesson Structure

1

What is World Heritage?

Whole class

Display 3–4 photographs of diverse World Heritage Sites without identifying them: the Great Barrier Reef, Angkor Wat, the Galápagos Islands, and the Historic Centre of Rome. Students observe and suggest what these places might have in common. Reveal that all are World Heritage Sites.

Introduce the World Heritage concept: some places are recognised as having “outstanding universal value” – significance that belongs to all of humanity, not just one country. The UNESCO World Heritage Committee maintains the list. There are over 1,100 sites across the world.

Two categories: cultural heritage (places made by people – temples, cities, monuments) and natural heritage (places shaped by nature – reefs, rainforests, geological formations). Some sites are both (“mixed”).

Key question: What makes a place important enough to be protected by the whole world?

This concept introduction needs to be solid but not exhaust the lesson. The bulk of time goes to site selection and initial research.

Materials
  • World Heritage photographs 4 images projected
2

Site Selection

Individual

Students examine the curated site list of 10 World Heritage Sites. Each site has a brief one-paragraph overview on the site overview sheet. Students read the overviews and select their site.

Students locate their chosen site(s) on the world atlas. This is the first step of the investigation and tests whether atlas navigation skills from L1–3 and L10–11 are secure at a global scale.

The teacher circulates during selection, conferencing briefly with students to ensure: (a) selections are distributed across the list (not everyone choosing the same site), (b) support students have chosen a site with accessible resources, (c) extension students have chosen two sites that offer meaningful comparison potential.

Extension

Students choosing two sites begin noting initial points of comparison during their atlas work.

Support

Teacher conferences with students during selection. The observation sheet includes prompt questions to guide initial thinking. Ensure selections focus on one site with clear, accessible resources.

Materials
  • Site Overview Sheet printable template below, one per student
  • Paper atlas world map and regional maps
3

Initial Research

Individual

Students begin their initial orientation to their chosen site using the atlas and Google Maps.

Atlas work

Locate the site, identify the country and continent, note nearby physical features (mountains, rivers, coastline, deserts), and describe the climate zone using the atlas climate map.

Google Maps

Satellite or street view where available. Students observe the site and its surroundings. What does it look like? What is nearby? Is it in an urban area or remote? What can they see about how the site is used or managed from the satellite view?

Recording findings

Students record findings on a structured research observation sheet. Fields include: site name, country and continent, coordinates, type (natural, cultural, or mixed), physical setting (at least 3 observations), and initial questions (at least 2 questions they want to investigate in L17–18).

This observation sheet is the foundation for the research in L17–18. It is designed to be added to across multiple lessons.

Reference

Teaching Notes

▾

The World Heritage concept is new and carries some complexity. Key points for the introduction:

  • “Outstanding universal value” is the formal criterion. Use this phrase with students – it captures the idea that some places matter to everyone, not just the country they are in.
  • The distinction between cultural and natural heritage is important for the investigation. Some students will assume all heritage sites are old buildings. The natural sites (Great Barrier Reef, Serengeti, Galápagos) challenge this assumption.
  • Avoid spending too long on the UNESCO governance structure. Students need to know that an international committee decides, and that listing brings both protection and responsibility. The detail of how nominations work is not necessary for Year 5.
  • L3 place cards seeded several of these sites. Check the inquiry wall for existing student questions – they can be referenced during the introduction.

Materials & Resources

▾
  • Paper atlas: World map and relevant regional maps for each site (class set)
  • Google Maps: Pre-load satellite views for 2–3 of the most popular sites so they can be demonstrated if needed
  • World Heritage photographs: Four high-quality images for projection (Great Barrier Reef, Angkor Wat, Galápagos Islands, Historic Centre of Rome). Source from UNESCO World Heritage website or similar.
  • Site Overview Sheet: A4 double-sided with 10 one-paragraph site descriptions (see printable template)
  • Research Observation Sheet: A4 structured sheet with fields for atlas and Google Maps observations (see printable template)

Atlas Work

▾

Students use the atlas throughout this lesson, not as a separate activity. During site selection, they locate potential sites on the world atlas. During initial research, they identify country, continent, physical features, and climate for their chosen site. The atlas work should feel integrated into the research process. Teacher support for atlas navigation should be minimal at this point – students have been building these skills since L1.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Can students use the atlas independently to locate a global site?
  • Are site selections distributed across the list, or has everyone chosen the same site?
  • Do initial observations show geographical thinking (“it is near the coast and surrounded by mountains”) rather than surface-level description (“it looks cool”)?
  • Are the initial questions investigable and geographical in nature?

How to capture

Site selection and observation sheets are the artefacts. Teacher notes which students needed support with atlas navigation and which students chose sites without reading the overviews carefully. These notes inform conferencing priorities in L17.

What it tells you

Students who can locate their site, describe its physical context, and generate geographical questions are ready for independent research in L17–18. Students who struggle with location or produce vague observations will need structured conferencing in the next lesson.

Printable Templates

World Heritage Sites – Choose Your Investigation

Read each overview carefully. Choose the site (or sites) you want to investigate.

Great Barrier Reef
Australia Natural
The Great Barrier Reef is the largest coral reef system in the world, stretching over 2,300 kilometres along Australia’s northeast coast. It is home to over 1,500 species of fish, 400 types of coral, and many endangered species including dugongs and sea turtles. The reef is threatened by rising ocean temperatures causing coral bleaching, as well as pollution and coastal development. It was listed as a World Heritage Site in 1981.
Uluru-Kata Tjuta
Australia Mixed
Uluru (also known as Ayers Rock) and the nearby Kata Tjuta rock formations rise from the flat desert of central Australia. For the Anangu people, these are sacred places with deep cultural and spiritual significance stretching back tens of thousands of years. The site faces challenges from tourism management and climate change affecting the delicate desert ecosystem. It was listed in 1987 (natural) and 1994 (cultural).
Machu Picchu
Peru Mixed
Machu Picchu is a 15th-century Inca citadel built high in the Andes Mountains at about 2,430 metres above sea level. Its precise stone construction and dramatic mountain setting make it one of the most remarkable archaeological sites in the world. Over 1.5 million tourists visit each year, creating pressure on the fragile ruins and surrounding environment. It was listed in 1983.
Great Wall of China
China Cultural
The Great Wall stretches over 20,000 kilometres across northern China, built over many centuries to protect against invasions. Different sections were constructed by different dynasties, using materials ranging from packed earth to stone and brick. Many sections are deteriorating due to erosion, human damage, and lack of maintenance, while the most-visited sections face overcrowding. It was listed in 1987.
Serengeti National Park
Tanzania Natural
The Serengeti is one of Africa’s most famous ecosystems, known for the annual migration of over 1.5 million wildebeest and hundreds of thousands of zebras. The park covers about 15,000 square kilometres of grassland, savanna, and woodland. Threats include poaching, proposed road and railway developments that could disrupt migration routes, and climate change altering rainfall patterns. It was listed in 1981.
Galápagos Islands
Ecuador Natural
The Galápagos Islands sit in the Pacific Ocean about 1,000 kilometres off the coast of Ecuador. Their isolation allowed unique species to evolve, including giant tortoises, marine iguanas, and Darwin’s finches. Invasive species introduced by humans, increasing tourism, and illegal fishing threaten the islands’ delicate ecosystems. The islands were among the first World Heritage Sites, listed in 1978.
Angkor Wat
Cambodia Cultural
Angkor Wat is a vast temple complex built in the 12th century as the capital of the Khmer Empire. It is the largest religious monument in the world, covering about 162 hectares. The site faces threats from mass tourism, illegal looting, and groundwater extraction causing the foundations to shift. It was listed in 1992.
Venice and its Lagoon
Italy Cultural
Venice is a city built on 118 small islands connected by canals and bridges in a shallow lagoon in northeast Italy. Its unique architecture, art, and engineering have made it one of the most visited cities in the world. Venice faces serious threats from rising sea levels, flooding (acqua alta), mass tourism, and a declining resident population. It was listed in 1987.
Rapa Nui / Easter Island
Chile Cultural
Rapa Nui (Easter Island) is one of the most remote inhabited islands in the world, located in the southeastern Pacific Ocean. It is famous for its nearly 900 monumental stone statues (moai) carved by the Rapa Nui people between the 13th and 16th centuries. The site faces threats from erosion, climate change, and the challenge of balancing tourism with the preservation of Rapa Nui culture. It was listed in 1995.
Wet Tropics of Queensland
Australia Natural
The Wet Tropics stretch along the northeast coast of Queensland, covering about 894,000 hectares of tropical rainforest. This is one of the oldest rainforests on Earth, home to plants and animals found nowhere else, including the southern cassowary and the Lumholtz’s tree-kangaroo. Threats include climate change, invasive species, and development pressure on the forest margins. Aboriginal peoples have managed this landscape for thousands of years. It was listed in 1988.
