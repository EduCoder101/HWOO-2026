Lesson 8 · Human Features and Land Use in Sydney

Investigating how different parts of Sydney are used
Lesson at a Glance
  • Explicit teaching of four land use categories – residential, commercial, industrial, recreational – with 2–3 Sydney examples for each; connections made back to Roseville
  • Each pair assigned a Sydney suburb (inner, middle, or outer) and investigates land use using Google Maps satellite view
  • Pairs record findings on a structured sheet, categorising features as they work through their assigned suburb
  • Students create a hand-drawn land use map with a colour-coded key – their first time creating a key for a map (this skill recurs in L10, L12, and the summative task)
  • Gallery walk: pairs visit at least three maps from different suburb types and note one similarity and one difference for each
  • Closing discussion: What systems connect these land-use areas? Where Line of Inquiry 2 (systems and connections) enters the unit

Learning Intention

Identify different types of land use across Sydney and explain why they occur where they do

Success Criteria

  • Define and give examples of residential, commercial, industrial, and recreational land use
  • Explain why different land uses tend to occur in different parts of a city
  • Create a land use map of a Sydney suburb with a colour-coded key

Lesson Structure

1

Land Use Categories

Whole class

Open by asking: When you look at a city from above – say from a plane or on a satellite map – what different types of areas can you see? What is the land being used for? Take a few quick responses. Students may mention buildings, parks, roads, shops, factories. That is the starting point.

Introduce the term land use: how the land in a place is divided up and used. Introduce four main land use categories that geographers use to understand cities:

  • Residential – homes, where people live; usually houses close together or apartments
  • Commercial – shops, offices, shopping centres, banks, restaurants; where buying and selling happens
  • Industrial – factories, warehouses, manufacturing; where things are made or stored
  • Recreational – parks, sports fields, playgrounds, beaches, community spaces; where people go for leisure

For each category, show 2–3 visual examples from Sydney using projected satellite screenshots or photographs. For example, residential might show a suburban street from above (detached houses) and an apartment building area; commercial might show a shopping centre or the CBD; industrial might show a factory precinct or warehouse area; recreational might show a park, sports field, or coastal reserve. Encourage students to identify the clues in the satellite view: How do you know this is residential? What can you see that tells you it’s industrial?

Connect back to Roseville: Can you think of places near Roseville that fit each category? Where is residential near us? Where do you see commercial areas? Are there any industrial areas nearby? This anchors the new vocabulary to the familiar.

Note: This lesson introduces five new terms (land use plus four categories) – the highest vocabulary load in this batch. All must be explicitly taught with clear examples before students investigate independently.

Materials
  • Visual examples of land use categories 2–3 per category; teacher-sourced satellite screenshots or photographs, projected on IWB
2

Suburb Investigation

Pairs

Assign each pair a suburb from the pre-prepared list mixing inner, middle, and outer suburbs. Give pairs a suburb assignment list and the recording sheet (one per pair).

The Task: Using Google Maps satellite view, pairs investigate their assigned suburb and record the land use. They systematically explore the area, identifying different features and areas, and categorise each as residential, commercial, industrial, or recreational. They record findings on the structured sheet: Feature or Area | Land Use Category | Evidence from Satellite View.

Circulate and ask probing questions: How did you decide this area is commercial? What clues in the satellite view tell you it’s industrial? Are there any areas that don’t fit neatly into one category? The goal is to develop observation skills and reasoning – not just labelling, but explaining why.

Make paper atlas available for reference. Pairs can locate their suburb, note its position relative to the CBD and other landmark suburbs, and discuss whether inner, middle, and outer suburbs show different land use patterns. This connects L2–3 map skills to the current investigation.

Support

Provide a partially completed recording sheet with some features pre-identified. Narrow the investigation to a few key blocks or areas of the suburb rather than the entire suburb. Pre-identify one row to model how to decode from satellite view into categories.

Extension

Pairs identify areas of mixed land use (e.g. commercial ground floor with residential apartments above, or retail parks next to industrial). Ask: Why do certain areas combine categories? What advantage does mixing offer? Students add a “mixed” category to their key with a brief written rationale.

Materials
  • Suburb assignment list teacher-prepared, one per pair, printable template below
  • Recording sheet one per pair, printable template below
  • Google Maps device satellite view, one per pair
  • Paper atlas Sydney/NSW page, one per pair
3

Land Use Map

Pairs

Students create a hand-drawn land use map of their assigned suburb on blank A4 paper. This is the first time they create a colour-coded key for a map – a skill that recurs in L10, L12, and the summative task, so it is worth doing carefully.

The Task: Pairs draw a simplified outline map of their suburb based on their Google Maps investigation. They draw outlines of residential, commercial, industrial, and recreational areas using the information they collected on the recording sheet. They then colour-code each area using four different colours, one per category. Finally, they create their own key: a small box showing each colour with its matching category label.

Emphasise that the map doesn’t need to be detailed or to scale – it should show the spatial distribution of land use types. The key is the most important part: it must clearly match colours to categories and be labelled accurately.

Circulate and ask: Why did you choose to colour that area blue (commercial)? What evidence did you have from Google Maps? Listen for links between satellite view observations and colour choices.

Extension

Students identify and label areas of mixed land use on their map and add a rationale to their key: Why do these categories mix in this part of the suburb? What benefit does it provide?

Materials
  • Blank A4 paper one per pair
  • Coloured pencils minimum four colours, one per category
4

Gallery Walk and Discussion

Whole class

Display all land use maps on desks around the classroom. Conduct a structured gallery walk: each pair visits at least three maps from different suburb types (one from an inner suburb, one from a middle suburb, one from an outer suburb). As they visit, they note one similarity and one difference they observe between their own suburb’s land use and the others.

Closing Discussion: Bring the class together and ask: What patterns did you notice? Are inner, middle, and outer suburbs different? How? Take 3–4 responses. Then move to the systems-thinking question: What systems connect these different land-use areas? If someone lives in a residential suburb, where do they work? Where do they shop? Where do they go for recreation? How do these different areas depend on each other?

This is where Line of Inquiry 2 (systems and connections) enters the unit. Don’t formalise it yet – this is an opening conversation. The goal is to plant the seed: A city isn’t just separate land use zones; they are all connected. Residential areas need services, shops, and jobs nearby. Commercial and industrial areas exist to serve the residential areas. This thinking deepens in L9 and beyond.

Materials
  • No additional materials

Atlas Work

▾

During Stage 2, pairs use the Sydney/NSW atlas page to locate their assigned suburb and compare its position to others. Encourage them to note: Is the suburb inner (close to CBD), middle (commuting distance), or outer (beyond continuous built-up area)? The atlas also helps them see regional patterns of urban sprawl and settlement. In the closing discussion, students can observe whether atlas-level patterns match what they found in their satellite view investigations. This bridges local detail with regional context.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Can pairs accurately categorise land use from satellite view, or do they misidentify features?
  • Do they explain their reasoning (e.g. “This is industrial because we can see large buildings and parking lots”) or just label without evidence?
  • In the map and key: are four colours used correctly? Is the key clear and legible? Do colour matches between map and key align?
  • Does the map show a reasonable spatial distribution for the suburb type? (Inner suburbs should have more commercial/industrial; outer suburbs more residential space)
  • In the gallery walk: can students articulate similarities and differences between suburb types?
  • Do students make connections in the closing discussion, or do they see land use as isolated zones?

How to capture

Circulate during Stages 2 and 3, using a brief observation checklist with pair names. Note: (1) accuracy of categorisation, (2) quality of reasoning, (3) quality of key conventions. Collect all maps – these are your evidence for key-creation skill. Listen during the gallery walk and closing discussion for systems thinking.

What it tells you

If pairs struggle to categorise land use from satellite view, they will need more scaffolded image reading before L10 (NSW investigation). If key creation is weak (mismatched colours, unclear labels, missing categories), students will need more explicit support before the summative map task in L17–18. If students don’t make systems connections in the closing discussion, Line of Inquiry 2 will need deliberate revisiting and deepening in L9. Strong responses – accurate categorisation, clear keys, and systems thinking – indicate readiness for the more complex land use investigations in L10 and L12.

Assessment Note

The land use map and key are the primary collected evidence. Consider photographing maps or collecting them for your records. The recording sheets show reasoning process and are valuable formative feedback tools, even if not formally graded.
