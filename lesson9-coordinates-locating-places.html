Lesson Structure

1

Latitude and Longitude

Whole class

Begin with the globe analogy. Introduce latitude and longitude as two axes for locating places on Earth. If a physical globe is available, use it – the equator-as-belt, poles-as-top-and-bottom analogy is powerful when students can see and touch it.

Latitude: Lines that run horizontally around the Earth, with the equator as the starting line (0°). Distances are measured north or south of the equator: 0° at the equator, 90°N at the North Pole, 90°S at the South Pole.

Longitude: Lines that run vertically (north to south), with the Prime Meridian as the starting line (0°). Distances are measured east or west of the Prime Meridian: 0° on the Prime Meridian, 180°E and 180°W on the opposite side of Earth.

Connect explicitly to grid references from L3: You already know how to read two axes on a map to find a location: along the corridor, up the stairs. Latitude and longitude do exactly the same thing, but for the whole Earth. Latitude is like the “up the stairs” – it measures distance north or south. Longitude is like the “along the corridor” – it measures distance east or west.

Demonstrate on a world atlas or globe: Find the equator. Find the Prime Meridian. Show the four latitude zones: polar (90°–60°), mid-latitude (60°–30°), and tropical (30°–0°) – both north and south. Explain that coordinates are always written as (latitude, longitude), with direction labels: 33°S, 151°E is Sydney’s approximate position.

As a guided example, locate Sydney: Sydney is about 33 degrees south of the equator (latitude 33°S) and about 151 degrees east of the Prime Meridian (longitude 151°E). On your atlas map, find the 30°S latitude line, then trace east until you find the 150°E longitude line. Their intersection is very close to Sydney. Let’s check: can you see Sydney on your map near that spot?

Materials
  • Physical globe if available, for equator/Prime Meridian analogy
  • Paper atlas world map page, one per student
2

Guided Practice

Whole class

Teacher models locating a place by coordinates on the atlas. Work through three examples as a class, using familiar places to build confidence: London, Tokyo, and Cairo.

Example 1: London

Coordinates: 51°N, 0°W. Model the thinking aloud: London is 51 degrees north of the equator, so I find the 50°N latitude line and trace slightly north to 51°N. London is on the Prime Meridian – that’s 0° longitude – so I find where 51°N meets the Prime Meridian. There’s London. Have students find it on their own atlas at the same time.

Example 2: Tokyo

Coordinates: 35°N, 139°E. Tokyo is 35 degrees north of the equator. I find the 35°N line. Tokyo is 139 degrees east of the Prime Meridian. I trace east from the Prime Meridian until I find 139°E (or estimate: it’s between 130° and 140°). The intersection is Tokyo. Emphasise the logic: read latitude first, then longitude, find the intersection.

Example 3: Cairo

Coordinates: 30°N, 31°E. Let students try this one with minimal scaffolding: Where is 30°N? Where is 31°E? Find the intersection. Verify as a class.

After each example, ask: How does this feel different from grid references on a local map? (It’s the same principle, but the lines are global and measured in degrees rather than grid squares.) Why do you think knowing coordinates is useful for geographers? (They work everywhere on Earth; you can locate a place anywhere without a map name; they’re the same worldwide.)

Support

Provide a simplified world map with latitude lines every 30° and longitude lines every 30°, clearly labelled. Reduce the number of examples to two (London and Tokyo) and model more heavily – point, say the numbers aloud, and have students echo.

3

Coordinate Challenge

Individual

Students receive a printed coordinate challenge sheet with five coordinate pairs. For each, they locate the place in the atlas and record: coordinate pair, place name, continent, and one interesting observation.

The Five Coordinate Pairs

  1. 48°N, 2°E – Paris, France (Europe) – familiar European capital
  2. 34°S, 151°E – Sydney, NSW (Oceania) – familiar local anchor; southern hemisphere
  3. 1°N, 104°E – Singapore (Asia) – equator region; different continent
  4. 30°N, 31°E – Cairo, Egypt (Africa) – different continent; northern hemisphere
  5. 41°S, 175°E – Wellington, New Zealand (Oceania) – close to Australia; southern hemisphere

These five coordinates give a good geographic spread across continents, with at least one location near Australia and one on the equator, to reinforce the learning.

Circulate and observe: Can students read both axes? Are they confusing latitude with longitude? Can they find the intersection? Note any student who struggles with the two-axis reading – this may indicate a need to revisit L3 grid reference logic.

Extension

Once students have completed the five challenges, ask them to find coordinates for at least two capital cities they have studied or that interest them. They can use the atlas to estimate: find the city, estimate its distance north or south of the equator, and estimate its distance east or west of the Prime Meridian. Record the approximate coordinates and compare as a class.

Support

Reduce the challenge to three coordinate pairs (Sydney, Cairo, Wellington – familiar anchors and straightforward locations). Provide the simplified world map with 30° gridlines. Work through one example together before students attempt the others independently.

Materials
  • Coordinate challenge sheet one per student, printable template below
  • Paper atlas world map page, one per student
  • Simplified world map support students only, printable template below
4

Verification and Collection

Whole class

Bring the class together to verify answers. Use Google Maps coordinate search, projected on the IWB, to check each coordinate pair. Type the coordinates in the format (latitude, longitude) – Google Maps will jump to that location. This is engaging and reinforces the connection between atlas and digital maps.

Work through the five challenges, one by one:

  • Call out the coordinate pair.
  • Ask a student or group to share their answer: Where did you find it? What continent? What did you notice?
  • Type the coordinates into Google Maps to verify. Discuss: Is that the right place? Does the atlas match?
  • Students self-check their recording sheet and correct any errors.

This verification step provides immediate feedback and celebrates the learning – students see their atlas work confirmed by technology they use outside school.

Mid-Unit Checkpoint Collection

While the class discusses and verifies, or immediately after this stage, collect all L5–8 work from students. Designate a collection point (trays, folders, or a table) and have students bring:

  • L5: Written reflection: “Why do people choose to live on the North Shore?”
  • L6: Labelled Sydney map (suburbs, landmarks, regions)
  • L7: Annotated physical features map (rivers, coast, plateaus, plains)
  • L8: Land use map with key (residential, commercial, industrial, parkland)

This collection is not a formal assessment moment – students bring work forward while discussion continues. The L5–8 portfolio informs your understanding of whether Phase 2 concepts are secure before moving into Phase 3 (L10 onward).

Materials

  • Collection trays/folders for L5–8 work

Atlas Work

▾

Students use the latitude/longitude grid on the world atlas map to locate places with given coordinates. This is the first time students navigate a world-scale atlas map with coordinates – building on the grid reference logic from L3 but at global scale. The practice moves from teacher-modelled (Stage 2) to guided whole-class examples (London, Tokyo, Cairo) to independent practice (Stage 3 challenge).

Key focus: reading two axes simultaneously and finding the intersection point. Encourage students to verbalise the process: “I’m finding 30°N latitude first, then 31°E longitude, then looking where they cross.” This metacognitive narration reinforces the conceptual link to L3 grid references.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Can students read both axes (latitude and longitude) simultaneously, or do they confuse which is which?
  • Can students find the intersection of two latitude/longitude lines?
  • Do students understand why coordinates are useful – that they work globally and consistently?
  • Can students approximate the coordinates of a known place (e.g. Sydney, London) when given a map?
  • Do stronger students identify interesting geographic patterns from the coordinate pairs? (e.g. “Singapore is on the equator, but Sydney and Wellington are in the southern hemisphere”)

How to capture

Circulate during Stage 3. Watch for students who struggle with axis identification or intersection-finding. Note: Do they point correctly at latitude first? Do they trace the correct direction? Can they estimate when gridlines are not marked? The self-check in Stage 4 (Google Maps verification) provides immediate feedback and shows you which students have secured the skill. Keep the completed coordinate challenge sheets as evidence of independent performance.

What it tells you

This assessment reveals whether students have bridged from local grid references (L3) to global coordinates. If students struggle with axis reading, the L3 logic may need reinforcing – the principle is identical, just at a different scale. If students confuse latitude and longitude despite clear teaching, this is a common difficulty; consider using the mnemonic “latitude is horizontal lines” as a memory aid in future lessons. The Google Maps verification in Stage 4 shows you which students can transfer the atlas skill to a digital tool – important for L15 when they locate megacities using online maps.
