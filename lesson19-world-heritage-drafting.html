Lesson 19 · World Heritage: Drafting and Peer Feedback

Transforming research into a structured information report

Learning Intention

Investigate a World Heritage Site and produce an information report on its location, significance, management and threats

Success Criteria

  • Locate the chosen World Heritage Site on a map and describe its geographical context
  • Explain the cultural and/or natural significance of the site
  • Describe the management practices that protect the site
  • Identify and explain the threats the site faces
  • Produce a clearly structured information report using geographical vocabulary

Lesson Structure

1

Report Structure

Whole class

Introduce the information report structure. Display it on the board and distribute the report structure guide (printable template below). Walk through each section briefly:

Introduction: Name the site, say where it is located (country, continent, physical setting), and note whether it is cultural, natural, or mixed heritage.

Significance: Explain why this site is important. What qualities earned it World Heritage listing? Why does it matter to all of humanity?

Management: Describe how the site is protected. Who manages it? What systems are in place?

Threats: Identify what threatens the site and explain why each threat is damaging.

Conclusion: Tie the report together. What is the biggest challenge facing this site? What would happen if management failed?

Remind students that this is an information report, not a narrative. The register is formal and geographical: “The Great Barrier Reef faces significant threats from coral bleaching caused by rising ocean temperatures” rather than “The reef is in trouble because the water is getting too hot.”

Extension

Extension students also have a comparison section that comes after threats and before the conclusion. It examines similarities and differences in significance, management, and threats across both sites.

Materials
  • Report Structure Guide printable template below, one per student
2

Drafting

Individual

Extended individual writing period. Students draft their information report using their L17–18 research notes and the report structure guide. This is where research transforms into connected prose.

Students should write each section in order, using their research templates as the content source. The report structure guide provides prompt questions for each section to help students develop their writing beyond simple lists of facts.

Teacher conferences with students during this period, prioritising: students with incomplete research (who may need to return to their resource packs briefly), students struggling to begin (the conferencing question: “Can you tell me in your own words why your site matters?” – if they can say it, they can write it), and students whose writing lacks geographical vocabulary.

This stage needs at least 25 minutes of sustained writing time. Resist interruptions for administrative matters.

Extension

Extension students use the same report structure plus the comparison section. Their report is longer and more analytically demanding. Encourage them to draft the comparison section after completing the four main sections, not instead of them.

Support

Support students receive the report structure guide with additional sentence starters for each section (built into the support version of the printable template). Teacher conferences prioritise support students during the first 10 minutes of drafting to help them get started.

Materials
  • Report Structure Guide continues from Stage 1
  • L17 Research Template student’s own notes

3

Peer Feedback

Pairs

Structured peer feedback session. Students swap drafts with a partner. Partners should ideally be investigating different sites, so the reader approaches the report as a genuine audience. For comparative students, pair them with another comparative student where possible.

Each partner reads the draft carefully, then completes a peer feedback slip. The slip is structured around two prompts:

One strength: “Something your report does well.” Sentence starters provided: “Your report explains … clearly because …” or “I could picture the site because you described …”

One suggestion: “Something that would make it stronger.” Sentence starters provided: “You could strengthen the … section by adding …” or “I wanted to know more about …”

Partners return the feedback slips and briefly discuss (2–3 minutes per pair). Students clip the feedback slip to their draft. Both carry forward to L20 for revision.

Model the process briefly before students begin: show what a useful strength comment looks like (specific, not just “good job”) and what a useful suggestion looks like (actionable, not just “add more”). This is the first structured peer feedback on geographical writing in this unit.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Can students transform research notes into connected prose (not just copying bullet points)?
  • Are students using geographical vocabulary naturally in their writing?
  • Is the report structured clearly with distinct sections?
  • Is the peer feedback specific and actionable (not just “good job” or “add more”)?

How to capture

Drafts and peer feedback slips are the artefacts. Teacher notes which students could not get started, which students produced thin drafts, and which students gave superficial feedback. These notes inform conferencing priorities in L20.

What it tells you

Students who produce well-structured drafts with geographical vocabulary are on track for the final report. Students whose drafts are disorganised or vocabulary-poor will need targeted conferencing in L20. The quality of peer feedback is diagnostic for the summative task, where peer sharing (L23–24) is part of the process.

Printable Templates

World Heritage Information Report – Structure Guide

Introduction
Key questions: What is the site called? Where is it located (country, continent, physical setting)? What type of heritage is it (cultural, natural, mixed)?
Significance
Key questions: What makes this site important? What qualities earned it World Heritage listing? Why does it matter to all of humanity, not just one country?
Management
Key questions: Who manages the site? What conservation work is done? How are visitors managed? Where does funding come from?
Threats
Key questions: What threatens the site? Why is each threat damaging? What is being done about it?
Conclusion
Key questions: What is the biggest challenge facing this site? What would happen if management failed?
Comparison (extension only)
Key questions: What are the main similarities between your two sites? What are the key differences in how they are managed or threatened?
