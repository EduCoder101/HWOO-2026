Phase 2 · City

Lesson 6 · Sydney – A City of Places

Understanding Sydney’s regional structure from CBD to outer suburbs
Lesson at a Glance
  • Google Maps zoom sequence from Roseville to Sydney: teacher pauses at 3–4 zoom levels; students observe what appears and disappears at each scale, discovering that city-scale geography reveals patterns (regions, transport corridors, green belts) invisible at street level
  • Explicit teaching of Sydney’s structure: CBD (Central Business District), inner suburbs, middle suburbs, outer suburbs, Greater Sydney – use Google Maps to show the spatial arrangement radiating outward from the harbour
  • Key question: Why do you think the CBD is where it is? What physical features might have influenced that? (Seeds L7’s topography content without pre-teaching it)
  • Students label a blank Sydney outline map with regions (A3, one per student – this is the collected assessment artefact)
  • Atlas investigation: students locate Sydney’s harbour, river systems, national parks and western plains, adding these to their map
  • Class discussion comparing completed maps: which features appeared on most maps? How is Sydney’s structure different from what we saw at local scale in Roseville?

Learning Intention

Identify the major regions and features of Sydney

Success Criteria

  • Name and locate at least five Sydney regions on a map
  • Describe how Sydney’s regions differ from each other

Lesson Structure

1

Scale Shift

Whole class

Teacher leads a Google Maps zoom sequence from street view of Roseville to a whole-of-Sydney view. Pause at 3–4 zoom levels. At each pause, students observe: What can you see now that you couldn’t before? What has disappeared? The purpose is for students to discover that city-scale geography reveals patterns (regions, transport corridors, green belts) invisible at street level. This mirrors the L1 zoom but at greater scale and with more sophisticated observation.

Key observations to guide students toward:

  • At street level (Roseville): individual shops, houses, local parks
  • At suburb level: clusters of amenities, school locations, main roads
  • At regional level: clear bands of different land uses radiating from the city centre
  • At city scale: the harbour dominates; suburbs are arranged in rough rings; roads and transport corridors form patterns
Materials
  • Google Maps Sydney overview, pre-loaded on IWB/projector
2

Sydney’s Structure

Whole class

Explicit teaching of Sydney’s regional structure. Define and show on Google Maps the five zones:

  • CBD (Central Business District) – Sydney city centre, where the Circular Quay, Central Station, and major office towers cluster. The concentrated zone of administration, commerce, and services. High-rise buildings, no residential sprawl.
  • Inner Suburbs – Paddington, Surry Hills, Marrickville, Strathfield, North Sydney. Within 5–10km of the CBD. Older, established residential areas with some mixed residential-commercial zones. Good public transport.
  • Middle Suburbs – Chatswood, Parramatta, Bankstown, Zetland, Penrith. 15–25km from CBD. Newer suburban sprawl with lower-density housing, shopping centres, some secondary business districts.
  • Outer Suburbs – Campbelltown, Wollongong region, Newcastle fringe. 30–50km from CBD. Car-dependent, newer housing estates, light industrial areas.
  • Greater Sydney/Western Plains – Beyond 50km: rural properties, farmland, small towns, national parks.

Key question: Why do you think the CBD is where it is? What physical features might have influenced that? Guide students to notice the harbour and Parramatta River. This seeds L7’s topography content without pre-teaching it. Students should notice that the CBD sits on the harbour’s deepest water and is protected by headlands. This was strategically important for transport and trade in the past.

Use Google Maps to show the spatial arrangement: CBD at the harbour, suburbs radiating outward in rough concentric bands. Overlay or highlight the main transport corridors – the M4, the rail lines, the motorways – and ask: What do the roads tell you about how the city grew?

Materials
  • Google Maps same setup as Stage 1, now with labelled regions
3

Map Labelling and Atlas Investigation

Individual

Students receive a blank A3 Sydney outline map showing coastline and major waterways as faint guides but no labels. Task has two parts:

Part 1 – Region Labelling

Students label the five zones (CBD, inner suburbs, middle suburbs, outer suburbs, Greater Sydney/western plains) using the regional structure taught in Stage 2. They may refer to the Google Maps display (keep it projected) or use their own mental map. This tests their ability to spatially locate the regions based on the explicit teaching.

Part 2 – Atlas Investigation

Using the paper atlas (Sydney/NSW map), students locate and add the following features to their map:

  • Sydney Harbour
  • Parramatta River
  • Georges River
  • Lane Cove River
  • Ku-ring-gai Chase National Park (northern)
  • Royal National Park (southern)
  • Blue Mountains (western edge)

This is the assessed artefact – collected. Students should use coloured pencils to distinguish waterways and land features from regional labels. The atlas work is significant: this is the first time students are reading a city-scale atlas map, a significant step up from the local-scale maps used in Phase 1.

Extension

Students add brief annotations noting what type of area each region might be (residential, commercial, mixed) based on atlas and Google Maps clues. This previews L8 land use investigation. Push thinking: Why do you think the outer suburbs are newer and more sprawling than the inner suburbs? What might be different about how people built them?

Support

Use the support map with region names already labelled. Students focus on locating and adding waterways and landforms using the atlas rather than identifying regions from scratch. Pair with a stronger reader to model atlas navigation at city scale.

Materials
  • Sydney outline map (core or support) A3, one per student, printable template below
  • Paper atlas Sydney/NSW page, one per student
  • Coloured pencils students’ own or class set
4

Consolidation

Whole class

Class discussion comparing completed maps. Key questions:

  • Which features appeared on most maps and which were missed?
  • How is Sydney’s structure different from what we saw at local scale in Roseville?
  • Why do the river systems matter to Sydney’s structure? (They connect suburbs to the CBD for transport and define green spaces.)
  • Why do national parks matter? (They show the limits of urban sprawl and provide recreational pull factors.)

This stage doubles as formative observation – circulate and note which students accurately located regions, read the atlas correctly, and can explain the spatial patterns.

Materials
  • Students hold up their maps for display and discussion

Atlas Work

▾

In Stage 3, students use the Sydney/NSW atlas page to locate Sydney’s harbour, river systems (Parramatta, Georges, Lane Cove), national parks, and western plains. This is the first city-scale atlas work in the unit – a significant step up from the local maps used in Phase 1. Students must read contour patterns, identify physical features from symbols and labels, and locate features on a much larger territory. Circulate and support students who struggle with scale or symbol reading; this difficulty signals a need for more atlas scaffolding in L7.

Observational Assessment

▾

What to look for

  • Can students locate the CBD? Is it placed correctly at the harbour?
  • Are regions placed in roughly the correct spatial arrangement (bands radiating from the centre)?
  • Are waterways named and positioned accurately on the map?
  • Can students read the atlas at city scale, or do they struggle with the larger territory and symbols?
  • Do students use the Google Maps display to verify their labelling?
  • Can students explain why Sydney’s structure differs from Roseville’s?

How to capture

Circulate during Stage 3 and note students who struggle with atlas navigation at city scale – this is a new skill and will take practice. Collect all maps. Check for: (1) correct regional placement, (2) at least 5–7 waterways or landmarks named, (3) CBD correctly located at the harbour, (4) regions roughly equidistant from the centre. Take photos of 2–3 exemplary maps and 2–3 that show common misconceptions for your records.

What it tells you

If students can’t read the atlas at city scale, L7’s relief map work will be harder. Pair these students with a stronger mapper in L7 for atlas work. If regional structure isn’t secure (e.g. outer suburbs placed closer to the CBD), L8’s land use investigation will lack the framework for comparing inner/middle/outer patterns. Strong responses that include annotations about land use types and can explain spatial patterns indicate readiness for the more analytical work in L8–9.

Printable Templates

Sydney Outline Maps

Teacher-sourced maps required for this lesson

The outline maps for this lesson need to be sourced or created by the teacher, as auto-generated SVG maps do not produce print-quality geographic outlines.

Core Version (A3, one per student)

  • Blank outline map of Greater Sydney showing coastline and major waterways as faint guides
  • Compass rose included
  • No region labels – students label the five zones (CBD, inner suburbs, middle suburbs, outer suburbs, Greater Sydney/western plains)
  • Space for students to add features from atlas: harbour, rivers, national parks, western edge
  • Name and title line at the bottom

Support Version (A3, 6–8 copies)

  • Same base map as the core version
  • Five regional zones pre-labelled (CBD, Inner Suburbs, Middle Suburbs, Outer Suburbs, Greater Sydney/Western Plains)
  • Students focus on locating and adding waterways and landforms from atlas

Suggested sources: Google Maps screenshot printed to A3, school atlas photocopied to A3, or a publisher outline map of Sydney. A clean Google Maps view with labels turned off works well for the core version.
